# Elbow Pain: Medial vs Lateral - What's the Difference

## Overview

Elbow pain is one of the most common complaints among baseball players, particularly pitchers. Understanding the difference between medial (inside) and lateral (outside) elbow pain is crucial for proper treatment and prevention. **Little League elbow**, the most common injury affecting young baseball pitchers (ages 9-14), specifically refers to medial-sided pathology. However, lateral elbow problems, while less common, can be equally serious. Knowing which side is affected, what structures are injured, and how to prevent these injuries can save your season—and your baseball career.

## Anatomy: Understanding the Difference

### Medial (Inside) Elbow Structures
The medial side of the elbow includes:
- **Medial epicondyle**: Bony prominence on the inside of the elbow
- **Medial epicondylar apophysis**: Growth plate in young athletes
- **Ulnar Collateral Ligament (UCL)**: Primary stabilizer against valgus stress
- **Flexor-pronator muscle mass**: Muscles that flex the wrist and pronate the forearm

### Lateral (Outside) Elbow Structures
The lateral side includes:
- **Lateral epicondyle**: Bony prominence on the outside
- **Radial head**: Top of the radius bone
- **Capitellum**: Rounded end of the humerus
- **Extensor muscle mass**: Muscles that extend the wrist and fingers

## Medial Elbow Pain: The Throwing Side

### Little League Elbow (Medial Epicondyle Apophysitis)

Little League elbow is **medial epicondyle apophysitis**—irritation and inflammation of the growth plate on the inner side of the elbow. It's the **most common injury affecting young baseball pitchers**, typically ages 9-14.

#### What Causes It?

The throwing motion places **valgus force** that creates tension on the medial structures (medial epicondyle, medial epicondylar apophysis, medial collateral ligament) and compression of the lateral structures.

During throwing:
1. **Cocking phase**: Arm moves back, creating valgus stress
2. **Acceleration phase**: Maximal stress as arm whips forward
3. **Follow-through**: Continued stress as motion completes

This repetitive valgus stress pulls on the medial epicondyle where the flexor-pronator muscles attach. In young athletes with open growth plates, this can cause:
- Inflammation of the growth plate (apophysitis)
- Stress fractures of the medial epicondyle
- Avulsion injuries (growth plate pulling away from bone)
- UCL damage in severe cases

#### Spectrum of Medial Injuries

Little League elbow refers to a spectrum of injuries to the medial side including:
- Medial epicondyle stress fractures
- Ulnar collateral ligament injuries
- Flexor-pronator mass strains

#### Warning Signs of Medial Elbow Pain

**Early symptoms:**
- Pain on the inside of the elbow during or after throwing
- Tenderness when pressing on the medial epicondyle
- Decreased throwing velocity or accuracy
- Gradual onset of pain over weeks or months

**Advanced symptoms:**
- Severe pain that prevents throwing
- Swelling or visible prominence of medial epicondyle
- Loss of elbow range of motion (especially extension)
- Pain with gripping or wrist flexion

#### Risk Factors

Research has identified key risk factors:
- **Age**: Peak incidence ages 9-14 (open growth plates)
- **Pitch count**: Exceeding recommended limits
- **Poor mechanics**: Improper arm slot or trunk rotation
- **Year-round throwing**: No rest period
- **Playing multiple positions**: Catching and pitching
- **Pitch type**: Breaking balls before physical maturity

## Lateral Elbow Pain: The Compression Side

### Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD)

Osteochondritis dissecans can develop from **compression on the lateral structures** through repetitive pitching, resulting in poor blood flow and areas of bone death.

#### What Causes Lateral Elbow Pain?

The same valgus force that creates tension on the medial side creates **compression on the lateral side** (radial head, capitellum). This repetitive compression can cause:

- Damage to the cartilage covering the capitellum
- Disruption of blood flow to the bone
- Formation of loose bodies in the joint
- Osteochondritis dissecans (OCD)

#### Warning Signs of Lateral Elbow Pain

**Early symptoms:**
- Gradual onset of pain on the outer aspect of the elbow
- Pain often worse during the cocking and early acceleration phase
- Aching after throwing sessions
- Mild swelling over lateral elbow

**Advanced symptoms:**
- Catching or locking of the elbow joint
- Sharp pain with certain movements
- Visible swelling
- Significant loss of motion
- Mechanical symptoms (loose bodies moving in joint)

#### Key Difference from Medial Pain

Unlike medial epicondylitis (rarely seen in baseball), lateral elbow problems in baseball pitchers typically manifest as **compression injuries like osteochondritis dissecans** rather than lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow).

Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow) is more common in:
- Tennis players (backhand motion)
- Manual laborers
- Activities involving repetitive wrist extension

## Prevention Strategies for Both Sides

### 1. Pitch Count Limits and Rest

**USA Baseball/MLB Recommendations:**

| Age | Pitches/Game | Required Rest (Pitches) |
|-----|--------------|------------------------|
| 7-8 | 50 max | 1-20: 0 days, 21-35: 1 day, 36-50: 2 days |
| 9-10 | 75 max | 1-20: 0 days, 21-35: 1 day, 36-50: 2 days, 51-65: 3 days, 66+: 4 days |
| 11-12 | 85 max | Same as above |
| 13-14 | 95 max | Same as above |
| 15-16 | 95 max | 1-30: 0 days, 31-45: 1 day, 46-60: 2 days, 61-75: 3 days, 76+: 4 days |
| 17-18 | 105 max | Same as 15-16 |

**Additional Guidelines:**
- No more than 100 pitches per day (including warm-ups)
- At least 3 months off from competitive pitching per year
- No pitching on consecutive days
- Avoid showcases and tournaments with multiple games in short periods

### 2. Proper Throwing Mechanics

Work with qualified coaches to ensure:
- Proper arm slot and release point
- Adequate trunk rotation
- Hip and shoulder separation
- Balanced follow-through
- No "short-arming" or excessive layback

### 3. Strengthening Program

**Shoulder Stability and Rotator Cuff**
- External rotation exercises: 3 sets of 15 reps
- Internal rotation exercises: 3 sets of 15 reps
- Scapular stabilization: 3 sets of 12 reps
- Thrower's Ten Program

**Elbow and Forearm**
- Wrist flexion/extension: 3 sets of 15 reps
- Pronation/supination: 3 sets of 15 reps
- Grip strengthening: 3 sets of 10 squeezes

**Core and Lower Body**
- Rotational core exercises
- Hip strength and mobility
- Lower body power development

### 4. Flexibility and Mobility

- Shoulder internal/external rotation stretches
- Sleeper stretch for posterior capsule
- Thoracic spine mobility
- Hip mobility (particularly internal rotation)
- Forearm and wrist stretches

### 5. Monitoring and Communication

- Track pitch counts meticulously
- Report any pain immediately (never "pitch through pain")
- Communicate with all coaches (if playing multiple teams)
- Annual arm screening by sports medicine professional
- Consider biomechanical assessment

## Treatment Approaches

### For Medial Elbow Pain (Little League Elbow)

**Conservative Treatment (First-Line):**
1. **Immediate rest** from throwing (typically 6-12 weeks)
2. **Ice** after activities that cause discomfort
3. **Physical therapy** focusing on:
   - Shoulder and scapular strengthening
   - Flexibility of shoulder and forearm
   - Core strengthening
   - Correction of mechanical flaws
4. **Gradual return to throwing** following interval throwing program

**When Surgery May Be Needed:**
- Growth plate avulsion fractures
- Displaced fractures
- Unstable injuries
- Failed conservative treatment

### For Lateral Elbow Pain (OCD)

**Conservative Treatment:**
- Rest from throwing (3-6 months or longer)
- Physical therapy
- Activity modification
- Monitoring with imaging

**Surgical Treatment May Be Needed For:**
- Unstable lesions
- Loose bodies in the joint
- Failed conservative treatment
- Significant cartilage damage

## Return to Throwing Protocol

Never rush return to throwing. Follow a structured interval throwing program:

### Phase 1: Warm-Up Tossing (Weeks 1-2)
- 30-45 feet
- Easy tosses, no wind-up
- 15-20 minutes
- Every other day

### Phase 2: Progressive Distance (Weeks 3-4)
- Increase distance to 60-90 feet
- Light tosses, still no wind-up
- 20-30 minutes
- Every other day

### Phase 3: Increased Intensity (Weeks 5-6)
- 90-120 feet
- Moderate effort
- Add crow-hop mechanics
- 30-40 minutes

### Phase 4: Return to Mound (Weeks 7-8+)
- Flat ground throwing first
- Progress to mound at 50% effort
- Gradually increase pitch count
- Fastballs only initially

### Phase 5: Full Return
- All pitch types
- Game situations
- Monitor closely for any symptoms

## When to Seek Medical Attention

See a sports medicine physician if you experience:

- **Pain that persists** beyond 1-2 weeks of rest
- **Pain during daily activities** (not just throwing)
- **Visible swelling** or deformity
- **Loss of motion** compared to opposite elbow
- **Popping, clicking, or locking** of the elbow
- **Numbness or tingling** in hand or fingers
- **Any pain in youth athletes** (should be evaluated promptly)

## Key Takeaways

- **Medial elbow pain** (inside) is most common in baseball, involving tension injuries to the UCL, medial epicondyle, and flexor muscles
- **Lateral elbow pain** (outside) involves compression injuries like osteochondritis dissecans, not typically lateral epicondylitis
- **Little League elbow** specifically refers to medial epicondyle apophysitis in young throwers
- **Valgus stress** during throwing creates tension medially and compression laterally
- **Pitch count limits and rest days** are the most important prevention strategies
- **Never pitch through pain**—early recognition and rest prevent serious injury
- **3 months off from competitive pitching** per year is essential
- **Breaking balls should wait** until physical maturity and proper fastball mechanics
