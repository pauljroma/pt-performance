# Command Development: Hitting Your Spots

## Quick Takeaways

- Command (hitting spots) differs from control (throwing strikes)—elite pitchers possess both
- High-accuracy pitchers show less lead knee flexion (30.6° vs 40.1°) and reduced arm kinetics
- Professional pitchers with better command experience decreased throwing arm stress
- Release point consistency, especially horizontal, correlates strongly with command
- Mechanical consistency throughout the delivery determines command more than any single adjustment

## Introduction

Command represents the pinnacle of pitching development—the ability to place the ball precisely where intended, not just in the strike zone, but hitting the catcher's target "seemingly at will." While often confused with control, command is a more refined skill that separates good pitchers from elite performers.

According to Hall of Fame pitcher Curt Schilling, "Control is the ability to throw strikes, and command is the ability to throw quality strikes." Research on professional pitchers has revealed that high command doesn't just improve performance—it also reduces injury risk through more efficient mechanics and reduced arm stress.

## Command vs. Control: Understanding the Difference

### Definitions

**Control**: The ability to throw strikes consistently
- Measured by walk rate (BB/9)
- Reflects strike-throwing consistency
- Doesn't account for strike quality or location precision

**Command**: The ability to hit specific targets within the strike zone
- Measured by location relative to intended target
- Reflects pitch placement precision
- Accounts for pitch quality and execution

A pitcher with control can avoid walking batters. A pitcher with command can execute specific pitch sequences, hitting edges of the zone and avoiding hard contact even when throwing strikes.

### Why Command Matters More

Research using Baseball Info Solutions data, which began tracking catcher target locations in 2010, enables breakdown of pitcher performance at unprecedented detail. Pitchers with superior command demonstrate:

- Higher strikeout rates (especially called strikes on the edges)
- Lower home run rates (avoiding hittable locations)
- Better performance with runners in scoring position
- Improved ability to execute game plans
- Reduced stress counts (3-2, 3-1)

## Biomechanical Foundations of Command

### The Lead Knee Position Finding

A landmark study analyzing 121 professional baseball pitchers found that **high-accuracy pitchers experienced decreased throwing arm kinetics**. Specifically:

**Lead Knee Flexion at Ball Release**:
- High-accuracy group: 30.6° knee flexion
- Low-accuracy group: 40.1° knee flexion
- The difference was statistically significant

**Arm Stress Differences**:
- Peak normalized shoulder internal rotation torque: significantly greater in low-accuracy group
- Normalized elbow varus torque: significantly greater in low-accuracy group
- Normalized elbow medial force: significantly greater in low-accuracy group

**Velocity Parity**:
- No significant difference in ball velocity between groups
- Better command doesn't require sacrificing velocity

These findings suggest that more efficient front leg mechanics (less knee flexion at release) allow for better command while reducing arm stress—a win-win for performance and longevity.

### Release Point Consistency

Release point variability directly affects command. Research shows:

- Horizontal release point consistency most strongly relates to command
- MLB pitchers exhibit significantly less variability than Minor League pitchers
- Even 2-5 cm of horizontal variability translates to several inches at home plate
- Vertical consistency also matters but horizontal is more predictive

If the state at the time of release is always the same, the ball will always arrive at the same position. However, there is always variability even for skilled players—elite pitchers simply minimize that variability.

### Mechanical Consistency Throughout Delivery

Command stems from repeating the entire delivery, not just the release:

**Consistent Leg Lift**: Same height and timing creates rhythm
**Stride Length**: Landing at the same distance (85% of height)
**Stride Direction**: Repeatable foot placement (4-14 inches closed)
**Hip-Shoulder Separation**: Achieving similar separation at foot contact
**Trunk Rotation Timing**: Consistent sequencing with lower body
**Arm Path**: Following the same route through cocking and acceleration

Any variation in these earlier phases cascades through the kinetic chain, affecting final ball location even if the release point feels similar.

## Training Command: Deliberate Practice

### Intent-Based Throwing

Command improves through purposeful practice, not just volume:

**Every Pitch Has a Target**:
- Never throw without specific aim point
- Visualize the exact location before delivery
- Get feedback (catcher, video, tracking) after each pitch
- Adjust based on miss pattern, not random changes

**Quality Over Quantity**:
- 30 focused bullpen pitches beat 100 mindless throws
- Rest adequately between pitches to maintain focus
- Film sessions to review mechanical consistency
- Track command metrics systematically

### Progressive Targeting

Build command through increasingly difficult challenges:

**Phase 1 - Zone Thirds**:
- Aim for general locations (up, down, in, out)
- Success rate should exceed 70% before progressing
- Focus on strike-throwing consistency

**Phase 2 - Quadrants**:
- Divide zone into four quadrants
- Aim for specific quadrant, track success rate
- Target 60% success before advancing

**Phase 3 - Specific Locations**:
- Aim for precise spots (low-away corner, high-inside)
- Track distance from target, not just hit/miss
- Elite command is hitting within 4-6 inches of target

**Phase 4 - Pitch Sequencing**:
- Combine multiple pitches to specific locations
- Practice game-realistic sequences
- Execute under simulated pressure

### Feedback Systems

Immediate, accurate feedback accelerates command development:

**Catcher Feedback**:
- Hold target until ball arrives
- Provide distance/direction miss information
- Establish communication signals for adjustments

**Video Review**:
- Overlay video to show release point consistency
- Compare high-command pitches to misses
- Identify mechanical breakdown patterns

**Technology**:
- Rapsodo, TrackMan, or similar for precise location data
- Track command metrics over time
- Correlate mechanics with location outcomes

## Mental Aspects of Command

### Visualization and Focus

Elite command requires mental precision:

**Pre-Pitch Routine**:
- See the exact target location
- Visualize the ball's path to that spot
- Feel the mechanics that produce that result
- Execute with conviction and trust

**Target Clarity**:
- The more specific the mental target, the better the outcome
- "Low and away" is inferior to "six inches off the outside corner, knee-high"
- Elite pitchers can visualize targets within 2-3 inches

### Dealing with Misses

Command development requires healthy miss management:

**Analyze, Don't Agonize**:
- Identify why the miss occurred (mechanical or execution)
- Make appropriate adjustment
- Throw the next pitch without dwelling

**Pattern Recognition**:
- Track miss patterns over bullpen sessions
- Consistent misses indicate mechanical issues
- Random misses suggest execution or focus issues

**Trust Your Stuff**:
- Mechanical overthinking during execution hurts command
- Develop mechanics in practice; trust them in performance
- Paralysis by analysis destroys feel and rhythm

## Pitch-Type Command

### Fastball Command

The foundation of all command development:

- Master fastball location before adding secondary pitches
- Practice commanding to both sides of the plate
- Develop comfort at multiple heights
- Four-seam vs. two-seam command may differ—practice both

### Secondary Pitch Command

Breaking balls and changeups require different command approaches:

**Curveball**:
- Tighter command tolerance (harder to spot precisely)
- Focus on down in zone vs. specific spots initially
- Arm-side vs. glove-side command often differs

**Slider**:
- Can command similarly to fastball if grip/release consistent
- Practice starting in zone and finishing out (sweep)
- Back-door slider requires advanced command

**Changeup**:
- Deception depends on fastball arm action/release
- Command down in zone is priority
- Arm-side fade helps create margin for error

## Common Command Killers

### Mechanical Inconsistencies

**Rushed Tempo**: Hurrying disrupts timing and rhythm
**Inconsistent Balance**: Variable leg lift affects entire sequence
**Flying Open**: Open stride direction hurts command to glove side
**Inconsistent Release Point**: Variability compounds throughout delivery
**Poor Front Side**: Collapsing or pulling off reduces direction control

### Mental Factors

**Overthinking Mechanics**: Conscious control during execution disrupts natural delivery
**Fear of Contact**: Aiming instead of attacking leads to tentative mechanics
**Nibbling**: Trying to be too fine, especially with runners on base
**Lack of Conviction**: Second-guessing location mid-delivery

### Physical Limitations

**Fatigue**: Tired muscles can't maintain consistent positions
**Strength Deficits**: Weak stabilizers allow unwanted movement
**Mobility Restrictions**: Limited range affects repeatable mechanics
**Injuries**: Pain causes compensations that destroy command

## Command in Game Situations

### Adapting to Conditions

Command requirements change based on:

**Count**: More precision needed in hitter's counts (2-1, 3-1)
**Runners**: Stretch delivery may affect command differently than wind-up
**Fatigue**: Late-game command often deteriorates—recognize and adjust
**Weather**: Wind, rain, cold affect grip and command

### Adjustment Strategies

When command falters during games:

**Simplify**: Return to basic strike-throwing before refining location
**Check Mechanics**: Quick mental checklist of key positions
**Tempo Reset**: Use time between pitches to re-establish rhythm
**Pitch Selection**: Favor best-commanded pitches until feel returns

## Measuring Command Progress

### Quantitative Metrics

Track these statistics to assess command development:

**Command+** (if available): Advanced metrics from tracking data
**Strike Rate**: Percentage of pitches in the strike zone
**Called Strike Rate**: Strikes looking (especially on edges)
**Chase Rate**: Pitches out of zone that batters swing at
**Zone Rate by Location**: Success rate hitting specific zones

### Qualitative Assessment

Less measurable but equally important indicators:

- Catcher's confidence in calling pitches
- Coach's willingness to call specific locations
- Personal feel and consistency session-to-session
- Ability to execute in high-pressure situations
- Reduction in "mistake" pitches

## Long-Term Command Development

### Age-Appropriate Progression

**Youth (12-14)**:
- Focus on strike-throwing and general zones
- Build mechanical consistency
- Develop feel for different pitches

**High School (15-18)**:
- Refine to quadrant-level command
- Begin tracking command metrics
- Practice pitch sequencing

**College/Professional (19+)**:
- Master precision command within inches
- Develop advanced pitch sequencing
- Optimize individual command strengths

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a pitching coach or sports psychologist if:
- Persistent command issues despite mechanical soundness
- Mental blocks affecting ability to throw strikes
- Sudden command loss without clear physical cause
- Difficulty executing specific pitch types
- Need for advanced command training methodologies
