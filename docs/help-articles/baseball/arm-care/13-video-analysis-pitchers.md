# Video Analysis for Pitchers: What to Look For

## Quick Takeaways

- Modern markerless motion capture systems achieve professional-level accuracy using smartphones at 240 fps
- In-game video analysis shows greater mechanical variability than laboratory settings, emphasizing ecological validity
- AI-powered systems like PitcherNet enable accurate biomechanical analysis from low-resolution broadcast video
- Each stadium now has 8-camera markerless motion capture systems capturing movement at 300 Hz
- Video analysis is essential because the human eye can only perceive 32 frames per second

## Introduction

Video analysis has evolved from a luxury available only to professional teams to an essential tool accessible to pitchers at all levels. Modern smartphone cameras, combined with AI-powered analysis software, can now provide biomechanical insights that previously required expensive laboratory equipment and marker-based motion capture systems.

A groundbreaking 2025 study published in the Journal of Sports Sciences evaluated the accuracy of multi-camera markerless motion capture systems during high-speed baseball pitching, confirming that modern technology can deliver professional-grade analysis. Meanwhile, researchers developed PitcherNet, an AI system that enables accurate analysis of baseball pitchers' mechanics using low-resolution video from smartphones or broadcasts—technology now used by MLB teams including the Baltimore Orioles.

## The Evolution of Motion Capture Technology

### Traditional Marker-Based Systems

Historically, biomechanical analysis required:
- Reflective markers attached to specific anatomical landmarks
- Multiple high-speed cameras (12-18) synchronized precisely
- Controlled laboratory environments
- Expensive equipment ($100,000+)
- Expert technicians for data processing

While highly accurate, these systems lacked **ecological validity**—the ability to analyze performance in actual game conditions.

### Modern Markerless Systems

Recent advancements have revolutionized accessibility:

**KinaTrax**: Each MLB stadium now has an 8-camera markerless motion capture system with cameras permanently mounted around the playing field, capturing movement at 300 Hz. Recent advancement in markerless motion capture capabilities has greatly improved accessibility to in-game, high-precision motion capture data.

**Theia3D**: This system has expanded biomechanics offerings from pitching analysis to functional movement assessments. Athletes don't have to wear markers, which makes assessments easier to deliver and has increased overall throughput in pitching labs.

**DVS X-Ray**: Offers swift analysis of pitching deliveries through marker-less motion capture technology driven by an artificial intelligence position inference model, capable of leveraging video recorded at 240 frames per second or higher.

**PitcherNet**: This AI system enables accurate analysis of baseball pitchers' mechanics and performance using low-resolution video from smartphones or broadcasts, democratizing access to professional-quality analysis.

### In-Game vs. Laboratory Findings

A 2025 study found that **in-game data showed significantly greater variability** for maximum shoulder external rotation and elbow flexion compared to laboratory conditions. This finding emphasizes the importance of analyzing mechanics under actual game conditions, not just controlled settings.

## What to Analyze: Phase-by-Phase Breakdown

### Wind-Up and Leg Lift Phase

**Key Checkpoints**:
- **Balance**: Weight over drive leg without excessive lean back
- **Leg lift height**: Consistent from pitch to pitch
- **Hip closure**: Front hip rotating toward back hip
- **Hands position**: Together at consistent location

**What to Look For**:
- Drift backward reducing forward momentum
- Inconsistent leg lift height affecting timing
- Early hip opening during leg lift
- Hand separation beginning too early

**Camera Angle**: Side view (first or third base side)

### Stride Phase

**Key Checkpoints**:
- **Stride length**: Approximately 85% of body height
- **Stride direction**: Lead foot landing 4-14 inches closed
- **Forward momentum**: Continuous movement toward home plate
- **Hip-shoulder separation**: Hips beginning to open, shoulders staying closed
- **Hand break timing**: Simultaneous with lead knee downward movement

**What to Look For**:
- Shortened stride reducing power generation
- Open landing ("flying open")
- Delayed or premature hand separation
- Minimal hip-shoulder separation

**Camera Angles**: Front (home plate) and side views

### Foot Strike Position

**Key Checkpoints**:
- **Lead knee flexion**: Moderate flexion (research shows high command pitchers at 30.6°)
- **Pelvis position**: Approximately 33° open to target
- **Shoulder line**: About 15° closed to target
- **Elbow position**: Approximately 90° flexion
- **Shoulder position**: 90° abduction, 20° horizontal abduction, 45° external rotation

**What to Look For**:
- Excessive lead knee flexion (>40° associated with reduced accuracy and higher arm stress)
- Premature shoulder rotation (reduces separation)
- Arm not in proper position (late, rushed, or inefficient path)
- Lead foot positioning issues

**Camera Angles**: Front and overhead views essential

### Arm Cocking Phase

**Key Checkpoints**:
- **Maximum shoulder external rotation**: 170-180° in elite pitchers
- **Elbow height**: At or above shoulder level
- **Scapular position**: Properly retracted and stabilized
- **Trunk rotation**: Beginning but shoulders still relatively closed

**What to Look For**:
- Insufficient layback (<160°) limiting elastic energy storage
- Elbow dropping below shoulder (increased injury risk)
- Premature trunk rotation reducing separation
- Scapular winging or poor positioning

**Camera Angle**: Side view critical for layback measurement

### Acceleration Phase

**Key Checkpoints**:
- **Internal rotation velocity**: Should appear explosive
- **Elbow extension**: Rapid straightening
- **Trunk rotation**: Full rotation toward target
- **Glove-side action**: Pulling toward body to assist rotation

**What to Look For**:
- Slow or labored arm action
- Incomplete trunk rotation
- Glove-side collapse or sweeping across body
- Release point inconsistency

**Camera Angles**: Multiple angles for complete assessment

### Deceleration and Follow-Through

**Key Checkpoints**:
- **Complete follow-through**: Arm crosses body naturally
- **Balanced finish**: Weight on lead leg, fielding-ready position
- **Trunk flexion**: Forward bend allowing energy dissipation
- **Lead leg position**: Stable but not rigidly locked

**What to Look For**:
- Abrupt stopping before complete follow-through
- Falling off to side excessively
- Stiff, rigid lead leg
- Poor balance affecting fielding position

**Camera Angle**: Side and front views

## Release Point Analysis

### Measuring Consistency

Use video overlay techniques to assess release point variability:

**Vertical Consistency**:
- Mark release point on multiple pitches
- Measure variation (elite pitchers: <2.5 cm)
- Greater variation indicates mechanical inconsistency

**Horizontal Consistency**:
- More critical for command than vertical
- Elite pitchers: <5 cm variation
- Relates strongly to strikeout ability and home run prevention

**Technique**:
- Film 10-20 pitches from side angle
- Use video editing software to overlay clips
- Mark hand position at ball release on each pitch
- Measure spread of release points

### Pitch-Type Differentiation

Analyze whether different pitch types release from consistent points:
- Fastball and changeup should be nearly identical for deception
- Breaking balls may have slight variation but should minimize
- Significant differences telegraph pitch type to hitters

## Practical Video Analysis Setup

### Equipment Requirements

**Minimum Setup**:
- Smartphone with 240 fps slow-motion capability
- Tripod for stable positioning
- Adequate lighting for clear image
- Reference markers (for measuring stride length, etc.)

**Advanced Setup**:
- Multiple cameras for simultaneous angles
- High-speed camera (300+ fps)
- Rapsodo or TrackMan for data correlation
- Dedicated video analysis software (Dartfish, Hudl, etc.)

### Camera Positioning

**Side View (First or Third Base Side)**:
- Position camera at pitcher's height
- Distance: 30-40 feet away
- Captures: Stride length, arm path, follow-through
- Essential for most mechanical analysis

**Front View (Behind Home Plate)**:
- Position at pitcher's release height
- Directly in line with mound and plate
- Captures: Stride direction, release point, balance
- Critical for assessing "flying open"

**Overhead View**:
- Position directly above mound (if possible)
- Captures: Stride direction, hip rotation, foot placement
- Valuable but often difficult to achieve

### Recording Protocol

**Consistent Setup**:
- Use same camera positions each session
- Maintain same distance and angle
- Similar lighting conditions
- Record all pitches in bullpen session

**Immediate Review**:
- Review key pitches between sets
- Provide immediate feedback
- Make micro-adjustments based on what you see
- Compare to reference video of good mechanics

## Analysis Workflow

### Step 1: Baseline Establishment

**First Session**:
- Record complete bullpen from multiple angles
- Identify 3-5 "best" pitches based on feel, velocity, and command
- Analyze these pitches for optimal positions
- Create reference standard for comparison

### Step 2: Identify Inconsistencies

**Compare Pitches**:
- Overlay "good" pitches with "bad" pitches
- Identify where mechanics diverge
- Note timing differences
- Measure position differences quantitatively when possible

### Step 3: Targeted Correction

**Focus on Key Issues**:
- Address 1-2 mechanical elements per session
- Use drills specific to identified issues
- Film drill work to verify correct execution
- Re-film full delivery to assess transfer

### Step 4: Progress Tracking

**Longitudinal Analysis**:
- Save and organize video by date
- Compare across weeks and months
- Document mechanical changes over time
- Correlate with performance metrics (velocity, command, injury)

## Advanced Analysis Techniques

### Overlay Comparison

Modern video software allows:
- Overlaying multiple pitches simultaneously
- Highlighting differences in position/timing
- Creating side-by-side comparisons
- Drawing lines and angles for measurement

### Slow-Motion Analysis

Since the human eye can only see 32 fps, slow-motion is essential:
- 240 fps allows frame-by-frame analysis
- Identify subtle mechanical flaws invisible at game speed
- Measure positions at critical timepoints
- Verify achievement of optimal positions

### Automated AI Analysis

Emerging tools provide:
- Automatic joint tracking without markers
- Velocity and angle measurements
- Comparison to professional benchmarks
- Injury risk assessment algorithms

## Interpreting Results

### Correlation with Performance

Video analysis should connect to outcomes:
- Does mechanical change improve velocity?
- Does consistency improve command?
- Does adjustment reduce pain or discomfort?
- Does new position enhance pitch movement?

### Individual vs. Universal Standards

Balance research-based ideals with individual variation:
- Not all elite pitchers match "optimal" positions
- Physical differences (height, limb length, flexibility) affect achievable positions
- Some deviations may be advantageous for specific pitchers
- Injury-free longevity suggests individual mechanics are sufficient

### Red Flags for Injury Risk

Certain mechanical patterns warrant attention:

**High Risk**:
- Inverted W arm action
- Significant elbow drop during cocking
- Excessive lead knee flexion at release (>45°)
- Severe flying open pattern
- Scapular dysfunction visible on video

**Moderate Risk**:
- Inconsistent release point (high variability)
- Shortened stride (<80% of height)
- Minimal hip-shoulder separation
- Incomplete follow-through

## Integrating Data with Video

### Combining Technologies

Maximum insight comes from correlating:

**Video + Velocity Data**:
- Identify mechanics associated with peak velocity
- Understand what changes when velocity decreases
- Optimize for velocity while maintaining health

**Video + Location Data**:
- Connect release point consistency to command
- Identify mechanical causes of location misses
- Refine positions for optimal accuracy

**Video + Spin/Movement Data**:
- Understand grip and release effects on spin
- Optimize pitch design through mechanical adjustments
- Verify execution of intended pitch characteristics

## Common Video Analysis Mistakes

**Over-Analysis Paralysis**: Analyzing too many variables simultaneously
**Ignoring Feel**: Relying solely on video without pitcher input
**Unrealistic Standards**: Trying to match positions impossible for individual body type
**Inconsistent Setup**: Changing camera angles/positions making comparison difficult
**No Action Plan**: Identifying problems without implementing solutions

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a biomechanics specialist if:
- Access to professional motion capture systems
- Difficulty interpreting video findings
- Persistent mechanical issues despite video analysis
- Injury concerns requiring detailed biomechanical assessment
- Desire for comprehensive analysis comparing to professional database
