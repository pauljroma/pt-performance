# Return to Throwing After Injury: Progressive Protocol

## Overview

Returning to throwing after shoulder or elbow injury requires a systematic, evidence-based approach. Interval throwing programs (ITPs) have been used for decades to enable baseball pitchers to return to competition after injury or surgery by gradually applying load to the throwing arm. Modern research emphasizes individualized, data-based progressions that safely rebuild throwing capacity while minimizing reinjury risk.

## The Evolution of Interval Throwing Programs

### Historical Approach

Traditionally, interval throwing programs were based on personal experience and clinical intuition. Generic protocols applied the same progression to all players regardless of individual factors, injury type, or position demands.

### Modern Data-Based Approach

Research emphasizes that "a shift has occurred in recent years from generic programs to individualized progressions." Advances in understanding biomechanics and workloads of throwing allow for more modern, data-based programs to be developed.

Recent studies have focused on creating evidence-based programs where "the newly created ITP is more familiar to modern baseball pitchers while exhibiting a more gradual buildup of chronic workload than traditional ITP programs."

## Goals of Return to Throwing Protocols

### Primary Objectives

**1. Safe Return to Competition**
Enable return "as safely and efficiently as possible, and potentially with less risk of setbacks or reinjury."

**2. Gradual Tissue Loading**
Progressive application of stress allows tissue adaptation without overwhelming healing capacity.

**3. Workload Management**
Modern programs emphasize monitoring acute:chronic workload ratios (ACWR) to minimize injury risk during rehabilitation.

**4. Restoration of Function**
Return to pre-injury throwing velocity, accuracy, and volume.

**5. Psychological Confidence**
Systematic progression rebuilds mental trust in the injured arm.

## Conditions Requiring Interval Throwing Programs

Research indicates ITPs may be used following common injuries or surgeries to the throwing shoulder and elbow:

### Elbow Injuries
- Tommy John surgery (UCL reconstruction)
- UCL sprains or partial tears
- Flexor-pronator strains
- Ulnar nerve issues
- Elbow stress fractures

### Shoulder Injuries
- Labral repairs
- Rotator cuff repairs
- Shoulder stabilization surgery
- Shoulder impingement syndrome
- Rotator cuff tendinitis

### General Applications
- Post-surgical rehabilitation
- Extended rest periods (>6 weeks)
- Recovery from significant injury
- Seasonal transitions requiring gradual ramp-up

## Phase 1: Preparation (Before Throwing)

Before initiating throwing, establish readiness through medical and functional criteria.

### Medical Clearance

**Required Assessments:**
- Physician clearance with specific timeline
- Physical therapist evaluation
- Pain-free status at rest and with daily activities
- No swelling or inflammation
- Restored range of motion (compared to non-injured side)

**Shoulder-Specific Criteria:**
- Full passive range of motion
- 90%+ strength compared to non-throwing arm
- Negative impingement signs
- Scapular control demonstrated

**Elbow-Specific Criteria:**
- Full extension and flexion
- No valgus instability
- 85%+ grip strength
- Negative UCL stress testing

### Functional Prerequisites

Before throwing, demonstrate:
- Adequate scapular stability
- Rotator cuff strength (external rotation emphasis)
- Core strength and stability
- Lower body power and single-leg stability
- Cardiovascular fitness

**Testing Battery:**
- Prone horizontal abduction hold: 30+ seconds
- Side plank hold: 45+ seconds each side
- Single-leg squat: 10 reps with good form
- External rotation strength: 75%+ of internal rotation
- Push-up plus: 15+ repetitions

## Phase 2: Short-Distance Throwing (Weeks 1-2)

### Distance and Volume

**Week 1:**
- Distance: 45 feet
- Volume: 25 throws per session
- Frequency: Every other day (3-4 sessions/week)
- Intensity: 50% perceived effort
- Rest: 48 hours between sessions

**Week 2:**
- Distance: 45-60 feet
- Volume: 25-30 throws per session
- Frequency: Every other day (3-4 sessions/week)
- Intensity: 50-60% perceived effort
- Rest: 48 hours between sessions

### Technique Emphasis

**Focus Points:**
- Proper mechanics from first throw
- No pain during or after throwing
- Smooth, controlled delivery
- Opposite arm (glove side) mirrors throwing arm
- Full follow-through

**Red Flags:**
- Any pain during throwing
- Mechanical compensations
- Increased soreness 24 hours post-throwing
- Reduced range of motion
- Swelling or warmth

**Progression Criteria:**
Advance only if:
- All throws completed pain-free
- No increase in soreness
- Mechanics remain consistent
- No swelling or inflammation
- Subjective readiness (player reports feeling good)

## Phase 3: Progressive Long Toss (Weeks 3-6)

### Gradual Distance Increase

Research emphasizes "progressive, sport-specific regimens that gradually expose an athlete to the demands they will experience upon a return to sport."

**Week 3:**
- Distance: 60-90 feet
- Volume: 30-35 throws
- Frequency: Every other day
- Intensity: 60-70% effort
- Add 15 feet per session if tolerated

**Week 4:**
- Distance: 90-120 feet
- Volume: 35-40 throws
- Frequency: 4 times per week (2 days on, 1 day off)
- Intensity: 70-75% effort
- Throwing on a line (not arc)

**Week 5:**
- Distance: 120-150 feet
- Volume: 40-45 throws
- Frequency: 4-5 times per week
- Intensity: 75-85% effort
- May include 5-10 throws with arc

**Week 6:**
- Distance: 150-180 feet (position players), 180+ feet (pitchers)
- Volume: 45-50 throws
- Frequency: 5 times per week
- Intensity: 85-90% effort
- Build-up and pull-down included

### Volume Progression Principle

Never increase distance and volume simultaneously. Research on workload management shows gradual chronic workload buildup reduces reinjury risk.

**Safe Progression:**
- Increase distance OR volume, not both
- Advance distance first, then volume
- Take "down weeks" (reduce 20-30%) every 3-4 weeks
- Monitor acute:chronic workload ratio (keep 0.8-1.2)

### Monitoring During Phase 3

**Daily Tracking:**
- Maximum distance achieved
- Total throw count
- Subjective arm rating (1-10 scale)
- Soreness level (0-10 scale)
- Any pain or concerns

**Weekly Assessment:**
- Range of motion measurement
- Grip strength testing
- Velocity measurement (if available)
- Functional strength testing
- Medical provider check-in

## Phase 4: Return to Positional Throwing (Weeks 7-8)

### Position Players

**Week 7:**
- Add position-specific throws: 10-15 reps
- Maintain long toss program: 30-35 throws
- Total volume: 45-50 throws
- Frequency: 5-6 times per week
- Include ground balls, fly balls, relay throws

**Week 8:**
- Increase position throws: 20-25 reps
- Reduce long toss: 20-25 throws
- Total volume: 45-50 throws
- Frequency: 5-6 times per week
- Game-speed throws introduced (50% of total)

**Return to Competition:**
After Week 8, if all criteria met:
- Participate in simulated game situations
- Limited innings/plays initially (3-4 innings)
- Progress to full games over 2 weeks
- Monitor workload carefully

### Pitchers: Mound Throwing Initiation

**Week 7 (Flat Ground):**
- Fastballs only
- Distance: 50-55 feet
- Volume: 20-25 pitches
- Intensity: 75% effort
- Frequency: 2-3 times per week
- Continue long toss on alternate days

**Week 8 (Mound Introduction):**
- Begin throwing from mound
- Fastballs only
- Volume: 20-30 pitches
- Intensity: 75-80% effort
- Frequency: 2-3 times per week
- Focus on mechanics, not velocity

## Phase 5: Return to Pitching (Weeks 9-12+)

For pitchers, the final phase progresses toward competitive pitching.

### Week 9-10: Building Bullpen Volume

**Week 9:**
- Mound throwing: 30-40 pitches
- Fastball: 75%, Change-up: 25%
- Intensity: 80-85% effort
- Frequency: 2-3 bullpens per week
- Rest 2 days between bullpens

**Week 10:**
- Mound throwing: 40-50 pitches
- Add breaking pitches (10% of total)
- Intensity: 85-90% effort
- Frequency: 2-3 bullpens per week
- Simulate inning structure (15-20 pitch sets)

### Week 11-12: Game Simulation

**Week 11:**
- Simulated game: 45-60 pitches
- Full pitch mix
- Intensity: 90-95% effort
- Face live batters if possible
- Follow game-day routine

**Week 12:**
- Extended simulation: 60-75 pitches
- All pitches at game intensity
- Full between-start routine practiced
- Medical clearance for return

### Competitive Return

**First Outing:**
- Limit: 45-60 pitches or 3-4 innings
- Normal rest before next outing
- Monitor closely for 48 hours post-appearance

**Progressive Build:**
- Add 10-15 pitches per outing
- Progress to normal workload over 4-6 outings
- Full between-start protocols
- Continued medical monitoring

## Workload Management Throughout Return

### Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio

Research emphasizes monitoring ACWR during return to throwing:

**Safe Range:** 0.8-1.2
**Danger Zones:**
- >1.3: Too rapid increase (injury risk spike)
- <0.7: Deconditioning (also increases injury risk)

**Calculation:**
- Acute workload = throwing volume past 7 days
- Chronic workload = average throwing volume past 28 days
- ACWR = Acute / Chronic

### Technology Integration

Modern ITPs benefit from technology:

**Motion Capture:**
- Monitors mechanics throughout progression
- Identifies compensation patterns
- Compares to pre-injury baseline
- Guides individualized adjustments

**Wearable Sensors:**
- Tracks throw counts automatically
- Monitors arm stress and fatigue
- Provides objective recovery data
- Enables remote monitoring

**Velocity Tracking:**
- Radar guns or sensor-based systems
- Establishes returning velocity benchmarks
- Identifies fatigue or setbacks early
- Guides intensity progression

## Setback Management

### Recognizing Setbacks

**Pain Return:**
Any pain during throwing indicates setback requiring:
- Immediate cessation of throwing
- Medical evaluation
- Regression to pain-free level
- Identification of cause

**Performance Decline:**
- Velocity decrease >5% without explanation
- Mechanical breakdown
- Persistent soreness
- Reduced command or accuracy

### Regression Protocol

**Step-Back Approach:**
1. Stop current throwing level
2. Rest 3-7 days (depending on symptoms)
3. Return to previous tolerated phase
4. Spend extra time (1-2 weeks) at that level
5. Progress more conservatively

**Medical Consultation:**
Setbacks require medical re-evaluation to ensure:
- No re-injury occurred
- Healing remains on track
- Progression timeline may need adjustment
- Additional treatment may be beneficial

## Position-Specific Modifications

### Starting Pitchers

Require longest, most conservative progression:
- Minimum 12-week program for most injuries
- 14-16 weeks for UCL reconstruction
- Emphasis on endurance (higher volume tolerance)
- Between-start routines integrated early

### Relief Pitchers

Different demands require adaptation:
- Can progress slightly faster (10-11 weeks minimum)
- Emphasis on intensity over volume
- Multiple weekly throwing sessions
- Shorter recovery needs between sessions

### Position Players

Generally shorter timelines:
- 8-10 weeks typical for most injuries
- Position-specific demands integrated earlier
- Less total volume than pitchers
- Game situations simulated sooner

## Post-Return Maintenance

Successful return doesn't end the program:

### Continued Monitoring (Weeks 13-26)

**First 3 Months:**
- Track all throwing volume
- Maintain arm care routines
- Regular strength and mobility work
- Medical check-ins at 1, 3, 6 months

**Injury Prevention Focus:**
- Continue rotator cuff strengthening
- Scapular stabilization maintenance
- Workload management remains critical
- Address any minor issues immediately

### Long-Term Arm Care

Research supports ongoing injury prevention programs:
- Daily J-Band or resistance band work
- Between-start protocols (pitchers)
- Weekly strength training
- Annual medical screening
- Workload tracking permanently

## Special Considerations

### Tommy John Surgery

UCL reconstruction requires most conservative approach:
- No throwing: 4-6 months post-surgery
- ITP start: 4-6 months post-op
- Mound throwing: 7-9 months post-op
- Competition return: 12-18 months post-op
- Gradual progression absolutely critical

### Youth Athletes

More conservative timelines needed:
- Add 2-4 weeks to standard protocol
- Emphasize mechanics over velocity
- Reduce intensity levels
- More frequent medical monitoring
- Consider growth and maturation factors

### In-Season Injuries

Challenges of return during competitive season:
- Pressure to rush return
- Difficulty maintaining progressive volume
- Temptation to skip phases
- Competing workload demands

**Recommendation:** Complete full ITP even if season ends before completion. Rushing return leads to reinjury.

## Conclusion

Return to throwing after injury is not a race—it's a systematic process backed by decades of research. Modern interval throwing programs emphasize individualized progressions, gradual workload increases, careful monitoring of acute:chronic ratios, and sport-specific preparation. The goal is not just returning to play, but returning safely with minimal reinjury risk. Research clearly demonstrates that properly progressed ITPs enable safe, efficient return to competition following shoulder and elbow injuries. The key is patience, adherence to evidence-based protocols, and willingness to regress when setbacks occur.
