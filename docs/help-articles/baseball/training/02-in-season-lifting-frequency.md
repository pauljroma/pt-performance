# In-Season Lifting: How Much, How Often

## Overview

In-season strength training represents one of the most challenging aspects of baseball periodization. Players must maintain the physical gains achieved during the off-season while managing fatigue from games, practices, and travel. Research consistently shows that strategic in-season training preserves strength and power while supporting performance throughout the competitive season.

## The Science of In-Season Maintenance

A landmark study published in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* examined training frequency effects on strength maintenance in 21 trained pubescent male baseball players [1]. Players completed 12 weeks of preseason training 3 days per week, then were assigned to groups lifting 1 day per week, 2 days per week, or a control group with no training during the 12-week competitive season.

**Key Finding:** A 1-day-per-week maintenance program was sufficient to retain strength during the competitive season for pubescent male athletes.

However, research on collegiate and professional players suggests slightly different protocols. A systematic review and meta-analysis found that for well-trained populations, 2-3 strength training sessions per week optimally maintains strength adaptations while managing fatigue [2].

## Training Frequency Recommendations by Level

### Youth Baseball (Ages 12-14)
- **Frequency:** 1-2 sessions per week
- **Duration:** 30-40 minutes per session
- **Focus:** Bodyweight movements, basic strength maintenance
- **Timing:** At least 48 hours before pitching

### High School Baseball (Ages 15-18)
- **Frequency:** 2 sessions per week
- **Duration:** 40-50 minutes per session
- **Focus:** Maintain strength, basic power work
- **Timing:** Day after pitching, avoid lifting day before starts

### Collegiate Baseball
- **Frequency:** 2-3 sessions per week
- **Duration:** 45-60 minutes per session
- **Focus:** Strength maintenance, power development, injury prevention
- **Lower body work:** 2 days per week maximum [3]

### Professional Baseball
- **Frequency:** 2-3 sessions per week
- **Duration:** 45-60 minutes per session
- **Volume:** Reduce by 40-50% from off-season baseline
- **Intensity:** Maintain at 85-90% of off-season loads [4]

## Volume and Intensity Guidelines

Research supports a strategic reduction in volume while maintaining intensity during the competitive season [4]:

### Volume Reduction
- **Total sets per muscle group:** Reduce by 40-50%
- **Exercises per muscle group:** Reduce from 3-4 to 2-3
- **Total training time:** Decrease from 75-90 minutes to 45-60 minutes

### Intensity Maintenance
- **Load:** Maintain 85-90% of off-season training loads
- **Effort:** Keep reps 2-3 shy of failure
- **Power work:** Maintain explosiveness at reduced volume

### Frequency Optimization
Research indicates maintaining training frequency at 3-4 sessions per week (when possible) with reduced volume per session is superior to reducing frequency while maintaining volume [2].

## Sample In-Season Training Programs

### Option 1: Two-Day Split (Most Common)

**Day 1 - Full Body Strength (Post-Game Day)**
- Trap bar deadlift: 3 x 5 @ 85%
- Dumbbell bench press: 3 x 6 @ 80%
- Single-arm dumbbell row: 3 x 6 each
- Lateral lunge: 2 x 8 each leg
- Pallof press: 2 x 10 each side
- Rotator cuff circuit: 2 x 12

**Day 2 - Power and Mobility (Mid-Week)**
- Medicine ball rotational throws: 3 x 5 each side
- Box jumps: 3 x 4
- Push press: 3 x 4 @ 80%
- Weighted pull-ups: 3 x 5
- Hip mobility circuit
- Shoulder health exercises

### Option 2: Three-Day Split (Optimal for Collegiate/Professional)

**Monday - Lower Body**
- Back squat or trap bar deadlift: 3 x 5 @ 85%
- Bulgarian split squat: 3 x 6 each leg
- Nordic hamstring curl: 3 x 5
- Med ball rotational slam: 3 x 6 each side

**Wednesday - Upper Body**
- Bench press or push-up variation: 3 x 6
- Weighted chin-up: 3 x 5
- Landmine press: 3 x 6 each side
- Face pull: 3 x 12
- External rotation: 3 x 12

**Friday - Power and Conditioning**
- Power clean or jump squat: 3 x 3
- Med ball chest pass: 3 x 6
- Broad jumps: 3 x 3
- Anti-rotation holds: 3 x 20 seconds each
- Conditioning (if needed): 10 minutes low-intensity

### Option 3: One-Day Minimum (Heavy Schedule Weeks)

**Full Body Maintenance**
- Trap bar deadlift: 3 x 5 @ 85%
- Push-up variation: 3 x 8
- Dumbbell row: 3 x 6 each
- Med ball rotational throw: 3 x 5 each
- Core circuit: 10 minutes
- Mobility work: 10 minutes

## Timing Considerations

### Pitchers
- **Never lift heavy the day before a start**
- **Optimal timing:** Day after pitching (post-recovery)
- **Bullpen days:** Light mobility and arm care only
- **Between-start maintenance:** 1-2 full-body sessions

### Position Players
- **Post-game:** Light mobility and recovery work only
- **Day after games:** Ideal for strength training
- **Pre-game (game days):** Avoid heavy lifting; light activation acceptable
- **Travel days:** Bodyweight exercises and mobility when possible

## Managing Fatigue and Recovery

### Red Flags to Reduce Training
- Persistent muscle soreness beyond 48 hours
- Decreased throwing velocity or bat speed
- Sleep disturbances or increased resting heart rate
- Increased injury complaints or movement compensations

### Recovery Strategies
- Prioritize 8+ hours of sleep nightly
- Nutrition timing: Consume protein within 2 hours post-training
- Hydration: Monitor urine color and body weight
- Active recovery: Light movement on off days

## Historical Research Support

A groundbreaking 1978 study with Major League Baseball players divided athletes into control and experimental groups during the season [5]. The experimental group participated in a strength training program in addition to game activities. At season's end, members of the experimental group were significantly stronger than the control group, demonstrating that in-season training not only maintains but can improve strength.

More recent 12-week in-season programs for collegiate baseball players have shown improvements in throwing velocity and sprint performance when properly designed [3].

## Key Takeaways

1. **Minimum effective dose:** 1-2 sessions per week maintains strength; 2-3 sessions optimal for most levels
2. **Reduce volume, not intensity:** Cut total sets by 40-50% while maintaining 85-90% of off-season loads
3. **Timing matters:** Schedule training around pitching schedules and game demands
4. **Monitor fatigue:** Be flexible and adjust based on individual athlete response
5. **Consistency over perfection:** Some training is always better than none

## Programming Checklist

- [ ] Schedule 1-2 strength sessions minimum per week
- [ ] Reduce volume by 40-50% from off-season
- [ ] Maintain intensity at 85-90% of baseline
- [ ] Time sessions post-game or day after pitching
- [ ] Include injury prevention exercises every session
- [ ] Monitor athlete fatigue and recovery markers
- [ ] Adjust based on schedule demands (tournaments, travel)
