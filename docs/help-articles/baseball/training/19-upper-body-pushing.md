# Upper Body Pushing: Bench, Overhead, and Landmine

## Quick Takeaways

- Strong relationship exists between upper body pushing strength and limb speed in baseball
- Bench press develops pectoralis major, anterior deltoid, and triceps—key contributors to throwing
- Push press reduces shoulder injury risk compared to strict overhead pressing for pitchers
- Landmine press offers scapular-friendly alternative to traditional pressing
- Push-to-pull ratio should be 1:2 to maintain shoulder health in overhead athletes

## Introduction

Upper body pushing exercises have been controversial in baseball strength training, with some coaches avoiding heavy pressing due to perceived injury risk to throwers' shoulders. However, research demonstrates a strong relationship between upper body strength and limb speed, particularly in heavy pressing horizontally and vertically, which is crucial for sports requiring high limb speeds including baseball.

The key is not whether to include pushing exercises, but which variations to prioritize, how to load them appropriately, and how to balance them with pulling work to maintain shoulder health in overhead athletes.

## Research on Pushing Strength for Baseball

### Performance Benefits

The prime movers in the bench press—pectoralis major, anterior deltoid, and triceps brachii—are also primary contributors to throwing and striking force, suggesting a direct performance benefit for baseball athletes. Research published in 2014 in the American Journal of Sports Medicine demonstrated that pitchers who lost greater than or equal to five degrees of shoulder flexion had a 2.8 times greater risk of injury, which provides rationale for maintaining overhead mobility and strength through appropriate pressing variations.

### Shoulder Health Considerations

Many baseball players have been hesitant to bench press due to factors like magnitude of load or perceived injury risk, but no one exercise is right or wrong for the sport. The solution is selecting appropriate variations and managing volume/intensity around throwing schedules, not eliminating pushing exercises entirely.

## Horizontal Pushing: Bench Press Variations

### Barbell Bench Press

The traditional bench press remains valuable for building upper body strength in baseball players when programmed appropriately.

**Technique for Baseball Athletes:**
- Grip width: Slightly narrower than powerlifting (reduce shoulder stress)
- Scapular position: Retracted and depressed throughout
- Bar path: Slight arc from lower chest to over shoulders
- Range of motion: Touch chest or stop 1-2 inches above to protect shoulders

**Programming:**
- Off-Season: 3-4 sets x 5-8 reps @ 70-80% 1RM, 1-2x per week
- In-Season (Position Players): 2-3 sets x 5-6 reps @ 70-75% 1RM, 1x per week
- In-Season (Pitchers): Consider alternatives or very light maintenance only

**Modifications for Shoulder Safety:**
Almost every element of the bench press can be modified by changing range of motion, movement path, or making it single-arm. Consider:
- Incline bench press (30-45 degrees): Reduced shoulder extension stress
- Floor press: Limited range of motion protects anterior shoulder
- Dumbbell bench press: Independent arm movement, natural press path

### Dumbbell Bench Press

Often superior to barbell for baseball athletes due to independent arm movement and reduced shoulder stress.

**Advantages:**
- Each arm works independently (addresses imbalances)
- More natural pressing path
- Greater range of motion for muscle development
- Safer for solo training (no spotter needed for safety)

**Programming:**
- Load: 70-85% of barbell bench press equivalent
- Reps: 6-10
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 1-2x per week

### Landmine Press

The landmine press challenges upper body strength and scapular stability with the scapula going through upward rotation with similar stressors to a bench or overhead press, but with reduced shoulder stress.

**Setup:**
- Barbell in landmine attachment or corner
- Stand in split stance or half-kneeling
- Hold bar end at shoulder

**Execution:**
- Press bar up and away on diagonal
- Follow natural arc of movement
- Maintain core stability
- Control descent

**Programming:**
- Load: 25-65 lbs (barbell plus plates)
- Reps: 8-12 per arm
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 2x per week

**Baseball-Specific Benefits:**
- Scapular-friendly pressing angle
- Unilateral variation addresses imbalances
- Core anti-rotation component
- Can be performed year-round safely

## Vertical Pushing: Overhead Press Variations

### Push Press

The push press is superior to strict overhead press for baseball athletes due to reduced shoulder stress and improved overhead mechanics.

**Technique:**
- Bar at shoulder level (front rack position)
- Slight knee dip
- Explosive leg drive
- Press bar overhead
- Lock out elbows

**Programming:**
- Load: 75-85% of strict overhead press 1RM
- Reps: 3-5
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 1x per week (off-season only)

**Why Push Press Over Strict Press:**
Research shows that pitchers benefit from maintaining shoulder flexion mobility, and the push press teaches explosive leg drive transfer to upper body while placing less sustained stress on the shoulder joint compared to grinding through a strict press.

**Timing Considerations:**
Vertical pressing volume and intensity should be well planned around the athlete's current throwing and other training demands due to its overhead nature and typically greater taxation of the shoulder. Avoid heavy overhead pressing within 48 hours of throwing.

### Half-Kneeling Dumbbell Press

Excellent overhead pressing variation that reduces load while maintaining movement pattern.

**Setup:**
- Half-kneeling position (inside knee down)
- Dumbbell at shoulder
- Core engaged

**Execution:**
- Press dumbbell overhead
- Maintain vertical torso
- Control descent to shoulder

**Programming:**
- Load: 20-45 lbs per dumbbell
- Reps: 8-12 per arm
- Sets: 3
- Frequency: 1-2x per week

**Benefits:**
- Reduced stability base increases core demand
- Lower loads protect shoulders
- Addresses bilateral imbalances
- Safer for in-season training

### Landmine Push Press

Combines benefits of landmine pressing with explosive leg drive.

**Programming:**
- Load: 35-55 lbs
- Reps: 6-8 per arm
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 1-2x per week

## Push-to-Pull Ratio for Shoulder Health

Research indicates that the push-to-pull ratio for the upper body should be approximately 1:2 for overhead athletes. Traditional weight lifting programs often demonstrate a strong focus on pressing exercises with less concern for pulling movements, creating muscular imbalances within the shoulder complex that can lead to injury.

**Practical Application:**

If you perform:
- Bench press: 4 sets x 6 reps
- Then perform: Rowing variation: 8 sets x 6 reps (or 4 sets x 12 reps)

**Weekly Volume Example:**
- Horizontal pushing: 12-16 total sets
- Horizontal pulling: 24-32 total sets
- Vertical pushing: 6-9 total sets
- Vertical pulling: 12-18 total sets

## Sample Pushing Programs

### Off-Season Upper Body (Monday/Thursday)

**Monday - Horizontal Emphasis:**
- Barbell bench press: 4x6 @ 80% 1RM
- Superset with: Chest-supported row: 4x12
- Landmine press: 3x10 per arm
- Superset with: Face pulls: 3x15
- Dumbbell floor press: 3x10
- Superset with: TRX row: 3x12

**Thursday - Vertical Emphasis:**
- Push press: 4x5 @ 80% 1RM
- Superset with: Pull-ups: 4x6-8
- Half-kneeling dumbbell press: 3x10 per arm
- Superset with: Single-arm dumbbell row: 3x10 per arm
- Landmine push press: 3x8 per arm
- Superset with: Band pull-apart: 3x20

### In-Season Maintenance (Position Players)

**Once Weekly:**
- Dumbbell bench press: 3x6 @ 75% effort
- Superset with: Dumbbell row: 3x12
- Landmine press: 3x8 per arm
- Superset with: Face pulls: 3x15

### In-Season (Pitchers - Non-Throwing Days Only)

**Once Weekly:**
- Landmine press: 3x10 per arm
- Superset with: Inverted row: 3x12
- Half-kneeling dumbbell press (light): 2x12 per arm
- Superset with: Band pull-apart: 2x20

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Excessive pressing volume for pitchers:** The shoulder experiences tremendous stress from throwing. Heavy pressing volume compounds this stress. Prioritize pulling over pushing 2:1 or 3:1 for pitchers.

**Training through shoulder pain:** Unlike muscle soreness, joint pain (especially anterior shoulder) indicates a problem. Modify exercises or reduce load immediately.

**Neglecting scapular position:** Allowing scapulae to protract (round forward) during pressing increases impingement risk. Cue "chest up, shoulders back and down."

**Same exercises year-round:** Vary pressing exercises based on training phase. Off-season can include heavier barbell work; in-season should emphasize dumbbell and landmine variations.

**Pressing day before throwing:** Schedule pressing work 48+ hours before throwing sessions when possible. Residual fatigue impairs shoulder function and increases injury risk.

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a sports medicine professional if you experience:
- Anterior shoulder pain during or after pressing exercises
- Clicking or popping in shoulder joint with pressing movements
- Loss of shoulder range of motion, especially overhead reach
- Strength imbalances between arms exceeding 15%
- History of shoulder injury without clearance for pressing exercises
