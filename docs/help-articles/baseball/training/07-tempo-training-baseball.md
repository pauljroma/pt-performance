# Tempo Training: Controlling Speed of Movement

## Quick Takeaways
- Tempo refers to the speed of eccentric (lowering), isometric (pause), and concentric (lifting) phases
- Notation format: Eccentric/Bottom Pause/Concentric/Top Pause (e.g., 3/0/1/0 = 3-second lower, no pause, 1-second lift)
- Slower eccentrics (3-5 seconds) enhance hypertrophy and strength without compromising neural adaptations
- Fast concentric speeds optimize neural adaptations and power development
- Extremely slow tempos (>10 seconds per rep) are inferior for muscle growth and inappropriate for baseball power goals

## Introduction

Tempo training—deliberately controlling the speed of each repetition phase—is an advanced programming variable that manipulates time under tension, mechanical stress, and metabolic demand. While often overlooked in favor of sets, reps, and load, tempo significantly influences training outcomes and exercise difficulty.

For baseball athletes, tempo training serves multiple purposes: building eccentric strength for injury prevention, enhancing muscle hypertrophy during off-season phases, and teaching movement control. A 2024 study in Frontiers in Physiology examined tempo's effects on strength and hypertrophy, providing evidence-based guidelines for implementation. Understanding when and how to use tempo training optimizes your program's effectiveness.

## Understanding Tempo Prescription

Tempo is written in a four-number sequence representing seconds in each phase:
**Format**: Eccentric/Bottom Pause/Concentric/Top Pause

**Examples**:
- **3/0/1/0**: 3-second lowering, no pause, 1-second explosive lift, no pause at top
- **4/2/X/0**: 4-second lowering, 2-second pause at bottom, explosive lift, no pause
- **2/0/2/0**: 2-second lowering, no pause, 2-second controlled lift, no pause
- **X** indicates "explosive" or "as fast as possible" while maintaining control

**Example Exercise - Bulgarian Split Squat with 3/1/1/0 tempo**:
- Lower for 3 seconds into bottom position
- Pause 1 second at bottom
- Drive up for 1 second
- No pause at top, immediately begin next rep

## The Science: 2024 Research on Tempo

A 2024 study published in Frontiers in Physiology compared fast eccentric tempo (1s eccentric/0s pause/1s concentric) versus slow eccentric tempo (4s eccentric/0s pause/1s concentric) in squats over 7 weeks.

**Key Findings**:

Research consistently shows that for muscular hypertrophy, the most effective adaptations occur when fast concentric movements are combined with slower eccentric movements. This combination maximizes mechanical tension while allowing explosive concentric training.

**Hypertrophy Effects**: Repetition durations between 0.5 and 8 seconds are likely more effective for muscle growth. Extremely slow repetitions over 10 seconds per rep appear inferior for hypertrophy.

**Strength Development**: Both quick and moderately slow tempos appear to yield similar strength results. Extending the eccentric phase can enhance hypertrophy and strength without negatively impacting neural adaptations.

**Power Limitations**: Due to submaximal loading inherent to tempo training, it may not be appropriate during training phases targeting maximal strength, maximal power output, or high-speed training. When the eccentric phase is extended, it may limit power output in the subsequent concentric phase.

## Tempo Applications for Baseball

### Off-Season Hypertrophy Phase
**Goal**: Maximize muscle growth
**Recommended Tempo**: 3/0/1/0 or 4/1/1/0
**Rationale**: Longer eccentrics increase time under tension and muscle damage, both hypertrophy drivers

Example:
- Dumbbell bench press: 3/0/1/0 tempo, 4×8-10
- Bulgarian split squat: 3/1/X/0 tempo, 3×8-10 per leg
- Single-arm row: 3/0/1/0 tempo, 3×10-12 per arm

### Eccentric Strength Building
**Goal**: Build deceleration capacity and tendon resilience
**Recommended Tempo**: 5/0/1/0 or 6/2/1/0
**Rationale**: Extended eccentrics with supramaximal loads (100-120% 1RM on lowering only) build eccentric strength critical for arm deceleration

Example for Pitchers:
- Eccentric-only pulldowns: 5/2/0/0 tempo (partner assists concentric), 4×4-6
- Eccentric hamstring slides: 5/0/1/0 tempo, 3×6-8
- Negative pull-ups: 6/0/0/0 tempo (jump up, slow lower), 4×4

### Movement Quality and Control
**Goal**: Teach proper mechanics and body awareness
**Recommended Tempo**: 2/1/2/1
**Rationale**: Slow, controlled tempo in all phases ensures proper positioning and motor learning

Example for Beginners:
- Goblet squat: 2/1/2/1 tempo, 3×10
- Push-up: 2/1/2/1 tempo, 3×8-12
- Split squat: 2/1/2/1 tempo, 3×8 per leg

### Intensity Technique During Deloads
**Goal**: Reduce load while maintaining training stimulus
**Recommended Tempo**: 4/2/1/0 or 5/3/1/0
**Rationale**: Extended tempo makes lighter loads challenging, allowing recovery from heavy loading while preventing detraining

Example Deload Week:
- Trap bar deadlift: Reduce to 60% 1RM, 4/2/X/0 tempo, 3×5
- Bench press: Reduce to 65% 1RM, 3/2/1/0 tempo, 3×6

## Tempo Strategies for Different Exercises

### Lower Body Strength Exercises
- **Back/Front Squat**: 3/0/X/0 (controlled eccentric, explosive concentric)
- **Deadlift variations**: 3/0/1/0 (controlled lower after lift)
- **Lunges/Split Squats**: 3/1/X/0 (pause ensures stability, explosive drive)

### Upper Body Pressing
- **Bench Press variations**: 3/0/X/0 (time under tension with explosive drive)
- **Overhead Press**: 2/0/1/0 (moderate tempo, less shoulder stress)
- **Push-ups**: 2/1/2/1 (full control, great for motor learning)

### Upper Body Pulling
- **Rows**: 2/1/2/1 (squeeze scapula at top, control on return)
- **Pull-ups/Chin-ups**: 2/0/1/2 (pause at top emphasizes lats)
- **Face Pulls**: 1/2/1/0 (pause ensures posterior delt activation)

### Rotational Power (Medicine Ball Throws)
- **Explosive throws**: X/0/X/0 (maximum velocity, no tempo control)
- **Rationale**: Power development requires maximum intent; tempo control is counterproductive

## Practical Implementation

**Start Conservative**: If new to tempo training, begin with moderate tempos (2/0/1/0 or 3/0/1/0). Extremely slow tempos dramatically increase difficulty.

**Reduce Load Appropriately**: Expect to use 10-20% less weight with controlled tempos versus normal speed. A 3/1/X/0 tempo may require 15% less load than 1/0/1/0 tempo.

**Count in Your Head**: Silently count seconds during reps. "One-one-thousand, two-one-thousand" ensures accurate timing.

**Use Tempo Strategically**: Don't apply tempo to every exercise. Reserve it for specific exercises and phases where time under tension benefits your goal.

**Progress Systematically**:
- Week 1-2: 2/0/1/0 tempo
- Week 3-4: 3/0/1/0 tempo
- Week 5-6: 3/1/1/0 tempo
- Week 7-8: Return to normal tempo with heavier loads

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Using Tempo on Everything**: Applying slow tempo to all exercises creates excessive fatigue and may impair power development. Reserve tempo for specific exercises and goals.

**Neglecting Explosive Concentrics**: Baseball requires explosive power. Always keep concentric phases fast (1 second or "X") unless specifically training control.

**Extremely Slow Tempos for Hypertrophy**: Research shows tempos >10 seconds per rep are inferior for muscle growth. Stick to 3-8 second total rep duration.

**Poor Counting Discipline**: Inconsistent tempo counting makes training stimulus unpredictable. Be disciplined with your counting.

**Inappropriate Phase Selection**: If training power, don't slow the concentric phase. If training eccentric strength, don't slow the concentric. Match tempo to training goal.

## When to Seek Professional Help

Work with a strength coach if:
- You're unsure when tempo training is appropriate for your goals
- You need exercise-specific tempo recommendations
- You're experiencing joint pain that might benefit from tempo modifications
- You want to implement eccentric training for injury prevention
- You're designing advanced periodization with tempo variation
