# Eccentric Training: Controlling the Lowering Phase

## Quick Takeaways

- Eccentric training improves muscle strength and hypertrophy more effectively than concentric-only training
- Nordic hamstring curls reduce hamstring injury incidence in professional baseball players
- Muscles are 20-50% stronger during lengthening (eccentric) than shortening (concentric)
- Increased time under tension during eccentric phase elicits greater strength adaptations
- Eccentric emphasis builds muscle elasticity critical for injury prevention

## Introduction

Eccentric training—emphasizing the controlled lowering or lengthening phase of exercises—has gained significant traction as an effective training method that focuses on controlled lowering of weights during exercises, enhancing muscle growth, joint stability, and overall functional strength. For baseball players specifically, focusing on the eccentric phase strengthens the muscles responsible for generating power and controlling movements like swinging a bat or throwing a ball.

Research shows that eccentric training not only helps increase strength but also improves muscle hypertrophy (growth) and injury prevention by enhancing muscle elasticity. Understanding how to program eccentric emphasis safely and effectively is essential for maximizing baseball performance while minimizing injury risk.

## Understanding Eccentric Muscle Actions

### Three Phases of Movement

Every dynamic exercise involves three types of muscle actions:

**1. Eccentric Phase (Lengthening):**
- Muscle lengthens while producing force
- Example: Lowering from pull-up, descending in squat
- Energy absorbed and stored
- Creates most muscle damage (stimulus for growth)

**2. Isometric Phase (Transition):**
- Brief moment between eccentric and concentric
- Muscle length unchanged
- Elastic energy stored in tendons

**3. Concentric Phase (Shortening):**
- Muscle shortens while producing force
- Example: Pulling up, rising from squat
- Energy released
- Produces movement and power

### Why Eccentric Training Works

**Greater Force Production:**
Muscles are actually stronger during the lengthening phase than when lifting (concentrically). Research indicates eccentric strength can be 20-50% greater than concentric strength, allowing for supramaximal loading protocols.

**Enhanced Hypertrophy:**
The eccentric emphasis training method provides a unique external load methodology by slowing the lowering or eccentric-loading phase of an exercise. As a training stimulus, this increase in the time under tension elicits changes in the muscles that improve their strength, function, and size.

**Injury Prevention:**
Focusing on eccentric movements helps increase strength while improving muscle elasticity, which is critical for injury prevention. The controlled lengthening develops resilience to the rapid stretch-shortening cycles common in baseball movements.

## Eccentric Training for Hamstring Injury Prevention

### Research Evidence

A prospective trial documented a reduction in the incidence of hamstring injury in professional-level baseball players after the implementation of eccentric strengthening exercise. Nordic-type exercises have been shown to decrease the incidence of acute hamstring injuries in professional athletes by developing eccentric strength capacity at long muscle lengths—precisely where hamstring strains occur.

Hamstring strains are among the most common injuries in baseball, particularly during sprinting and base running. The eccentric strength of the hamstrings—their ability to control lengthening under load during the late swing phase of running—is critical for injury prevention.

### Nordic Hamstring Curl Protocol

The Nordic hamstring curl is the most researched eccentric exercise for hamstring injury prevention.

**Technique:**
- Kneel with ankles secured (partner holds or anchor)
- Keep hips extended, body straight from knees to shoulders
- Slowly lower torso forward using hamstrings to control descent
- Lower as far as possible maintaining control
- Use hands to catch at bottom
- Push back to start with hands, or perform concentric return if strong enough

**Progression Protocol (8-12 weeks):**

*Week 1-2:*
- 2 sets x 3-4 reps
- Focus on controlled eccentric (4-6 seconds)
- Frequency: 2x per week

*Week 3-4:*
- 2 sets x 5-6 reps
- Maintain 4-5 second eccentric
- Frequency: 2x per week

*Week 5-8:*
- 3 sets x 6-8 reps
- 3-4 second eccentric
- Frequency: 2x per week

*Week 9-12:*
- 3 sets x 8-10 reps
- Natural tempo
- Frequency: 2x per week

**In-Season Maintenance:**
- 2 sets x 6-8 reps
- Frequency: 1-2x per week
- Never day before games or sprinting sessions

## Eccentric Training Methods

### Tempo Training (Slow Eccentrics)

Deliberately slowing the lowering phase increases time under tension.

**Standard Tempo Notation:**
4-1-1-0 means:
- 4 seconds eccentric (lowering)
- 1 second bottom pause
- 1 second concentric (lifting)
- 0 second top pause

**Example - Eccentric-Emphasis Romanian Deadlift:**
- Load: 70% of normal working weight
- Tempo: 5-1-1-0 (5-second lower, 1-second pause, normal lift)
- Reps: 6-8
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 1x per week

**Programming Consideration:**
Reduce load by 20-30% when using slow eccentric tempos. The increased time under tension compensates for reduced load.

### Supramaximal Eccentrics (Negatives)

Using loads greater than concentric 1RM, focusing only on eccentric phase.

**Example - Negative Pull-Up:**
- Jump or step to top position (chin over bar)
- Lower slowly (5-8 seconds)
- Step down and reset
- Reps: 3-5
- Sets: 3-4

**Safety Considerations:**
- Requires spotter or safety equipment for barbell exercises
- Reserve for advanced athletes (2+ years training experience)
- Use sparingly (1x per week maximum)
- Expect significant muscle soreness

### Two-Limb Up, One-Limb Down

Using bilateral concentric, unilateral eccentric to increase eccentric load.

**Example - Leg Curl:**
- Lift weight with both legs (concentric)
- Lower with one leg (eccentric)
- Alternate legs each rep

**Programming:**
- Load: 60-70% of bilateral max
- Reps: 6-8 per leg
- Sets: 3
- Frequency: 1-2x per week

### Eccentric Accentuated Training

Adding load during eccentric phase using bands, weight releasers, or manual resistance.

**Example - Band-Resisted Squat:**
- Bands attached to top of rack increase resistance during descent
- Resistance decreases during ascent
- Creates eccentric overload

**Programming:**
- Use cautiously with qualified supervision
- Advanced athletes only
- 1x per week maximum

## Baseball-Specific Eccentric Applications

### Upper Body (Pitchers and Position Players)

**Eccentric-Emphasis Dumbbell Row:**
- Pull dumbbell to hip (1-2 seconds)
- Lower slowly (4-5 seconds)
- Load: 70% of normal rowing weight
- Reps: 8-10 per arm
- Sets: 3
- Benefit: Builds lat and posterior shoulder strength for throwing deceleration

**Eccentric Pull-Up:**
- Lower from top position in 6-8 seconds
- Reps: 4-6
- Sets: 3-4
- Benefit: Develops eccentric shoulder and back strength critical for controlling arm deceleration

**Eccentric Push-Up:**
- Lower to ground in 5 seconds
- Push up normally (or drop to knees if needed)
- Reps: 6-10
- Sets: 3
- Benefit: Builds chest and tricep eccentric strength with minimal equipment

### Lower Body (All Positions)

**Eccentric-Emphasis Bulgarian Split Squat:**
- 4-second descent
- Explosive concentric
- Load: 60% of normal split squat weight
- Reps: 6-8 per leg
- Sets: 3
- Benefit: Develops single-leg control and strength at longer muscle lengths

**Eccentric Step-Down:**
- Stand on box (12-18 inches)
- Slowly lower opposite foot to ground (4-5 seconds)
- Tap ground lightly, return to start
- Reps: 8-10 per leg
- Sets: 3
- Benefit: Builds eccentric quad and glute strength, improves landing mechanics

## Programming Eccentric Training

### Integration with Regular Training

Eccentric emphasis is supplemental, not the entire program:

**Option 1: Dedicated Eccentric Day**
- One training day per week emphasizes eccentric work
- All other days normal tempo training
- Allows focused recovery from eccentric stress

**Option 2: Exercise-Specific Application**
- Add eccentric emphasis to 1-2 exercises per session
- Keep other exercises normal tempo
- Distribute eccentric stress across week

**Option 3: Phase-Based Approach**
- 3-4 week block of eccentric emphasis
- Return to normal training tempo
- Repeat periodically throughout year

### Sample Programs

**Off-Season Eccentric Block (4 weeks):**

*Monday - Lower Body:*
- Back squat (4-1-1-0 tempo): 4x6 @ 70% 1RM
- Nordic hamstring curl: 3x6-8
- Eccentric step-down: 3x8 per leg

*Wednesday - Upper Body:*
- Bench press (5-1-1-0 tempo): 4x6 @ 65% 1RM
- Eccentric pull-up: 4x5 (8-sec lower)
- Dumbbell row (4-0-1-0 tempo): 3x8 per arm

*Friday - Full Body:*
- Trap bar deadlift (normal tempo): 4x5
- Bulgarian split squat (4-1-X-0 tempo): 3x8 per leg
- Face pulls (3-0-1-0 tempo): 3x15

**In-Season Maintenance:**

*Once Weekly (Non-Throwing Day for Pitchers):*
- Nordic hamstring curl: 2x6-8
- Eccentric-emphasis row: 3x8 per arm
- Single-leg RDL (3-second lower): 3x8 per leg

## Recovery Considerations

### Delayed-Onset Muscle Soreness (DOMS)

Eccentric training creates more muscle damage than concentric work, resulting in significant DOMS, especially when first implementing eccentric protocols.

**Management Strategies:**
- Start with lower volume (50% of normal sets/reps)
- Progress gradually (add 1-2 reps per week)
- Allow 48-72 hours before training same muscle group
- Avoid eccentric emphasis for 3-4 days before competition

**Repeated Bout Effect:**
After 2-3 exposures to eccentric training, muscle damage decreases significantly. Athletes adapt quickly, allowing increased volume over time.

### Timing Considerations

**Off-Season:**
- Best time for eccentric emphasis blocks
- Allow full recovery between sessions
- Can tolerate higher volumes and intensities

**Pre-Season:**
- Reduce eccentric volume as competition approaches
- Maintain with light eccentric work (Nordic curls, tempo work)
- Avoid new eccentric protocols within 4 weeks of season start

**In-Season:**
- Maintenance only (Nordic curls for hamstring health)
- Very low volume (2 sets per exercise maximum)
- Scheduled on light training days
- Never before competition or heavy throwing days

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Excessive volume initially:** Starting with high-volume eccentric training causes debilitating soreness that impairs performance for days. Start conservatively.

**Training through pain:** Muscle soreness is expected; joint pain is not. Modify or eliminate exercises causing joint discomfort.

**Neglecting concentric strength:** Eccentric training is supplemental. Continue developing concentric power through explosive exercises.

**Poor eccentric control:** Eccentric training requires controlled lowering, not dropping the weight. Maintain tension throughout entire range.

**Insufficient recovery:** Eccentric training requires longer recovery than concentric work. Avoid training same muscle groups within 48 hours.

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a sports medicine professional if you experience:
- Excessive muscle soreness lasting beyond 72 hours
- Joint pain during or after eccentric exercises
- Decreased range of motion that doesn't resolve with rest
- History of muscle strains without proper clearance for eccentric training
- Uncertainty about appropriate loads and progressions
