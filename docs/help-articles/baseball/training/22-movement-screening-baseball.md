# Movement Screening: FMS and Y-Balance for Baseball

## Quick Takeaways

- The Functional Movement Screen (FMS) and Y-Balance Test (YBT) assess fundamental movement patterns and dynamic stability
- Research on baseball players shows mixed results: FMS and YBT alone may not accurately predict injury risk
- FMS scores improved after 12-week training programs in high school baseball players, but returned to baseline after ceasing training
- Multifactorial models combining workload ratios, injury history, and fitness data provide better injury prediction than FMS/YBT alone
- These screens are most valuable for identifying movement deficits to guide training, not as standalone injury predictors

## Introduction

Movement screening—systematic assessment of fundamental movement patterns and dynamic stability—has become increasingly popular in baseball training programs. The Functional Movement Screen (FMS) and Y-Balance Test (YBT) are the most widely used tools, promising to identify injury risk and guide training interventions. However, recent research reveals a more nuanced picture: while these screens can identify movement limitations, their predictive power for injury is limited when used in isolation. Understanding both the value and limitations of movement screening is essential for evidence-based practice.

## The Biomechanics of Movement Screening

### Functional Movement Screen (FMS)

The FMS assesses seven fundamental movement patterns:

1. **Deep Squat**: Bilateral, symmetrical functional mobility of hips, knees, and ankles
2. **Hurdle Step**: Step mechanics, stability, and functional mobility
3. **In-Line Lunge**: Hip and ankle mobility, quadriceps flexibility, knee stability
4. **Shoulder Mobility**: Bilateral shoulder range of motion
5. **Active Straight Leg Raise**: Active hamstring and gastroc-soleus flexibility
6. **Trunk Stability Push-Up**: Core stability during a closed-chain upper body movement
7. **Rotary Stability**: Multi-plane trunk stability during combined upper and lower extremity motion

Each movement is scored 0-3, with total scores ranging from 0-21. Scores ≤14 have traditionally been considered increased injury risk, though this cutoff has been questioned by recent research.

### Y-Balance Test (YBT)

The YBT assesses dynamic balance and neuromuscular control. Athletes stand on one leg and reach with the opposite leg in three directions:

- **Anterior**: Reaching forward
- **Posteromedial**: Reaching backward and medially
- **Posterolateral**: Reaching backward and laterally

Composite reach distance, normalized to leg length, provides a measure of dynamic stability. Asymmetries >4 cm between limbs have been associated with injury risk in some populations.

### Research in Baseball Athletes

**High School Baseball Players - FMS Across Seasons (2017)**:
No previous study had examined FMS scores between preparative and competitive periods in baseball players. While FMS scores differed significantly between seasons, the low sensitivity indicated that FMS does not accurately predict injury risk, particularly during competitive periods.

**FMS and YBT Association Study (2019)**:
A study of 124 high school athletes (including 10 baseball players) found that FMS and YBT were not associated with increased risk of lower extremity injury. These findings did not support their use as stand-alone injury risk assessments in high school athletes.

**FMS Training Intervention (2021-2022)**:
A randomized controlled trial examined FMS training effects in high school baseball players. After 12 weeks, good movement patterns were acquired, and continuous training was considered necessary to improve movements and decrease injuries after 24 weeks. Critically, improved FMS scores returned to original levels 12 weeks after ceasing training, indicating athletes need continuous FMS training to maintain improvements.

## Practical Application for Baseball

### When to Use Movement Screening

**Appropriate Uses**:

1. **Baseline Assessment**: Establish individual movement quality profiles
2. **Training Needs Analysis**: Identify specific movement limitations requiring attention
3. **Progress Monitoring**: Track changes in movement quality over training cycles
4. **Return-to-Sport**: Ensure movement quality restoration after injury
5. **Program Design**: Guide exercise selection based on identified deficits

**Inappropriate Uses**:

1. **Sole Injury Predictor**: Don't make participation decisions based solely on FMS/YBT scores
2. **Replacement for Medical Evaluation**: Screens don't diagnose injuries or medical conditions
3. **One-Time Testing**: Single assessments have limited value without context or follow-up
4. **Universal Cutoff Scores**: Individual baselines and changes over time matter more than population cutoffs

### Implementing Screening in Baseball Programs

**Pre-Season Assessment**:
- Conduct FMS and YBT during pre-season physicals
- Establish individual baselines for each athlete
- Identify movement patterns requiring specific intervention
- Design individualized training programs targeting deficits

**In-Season Monitoring**:
- Simplified movement checks (not full FMS battery)
- Focus on patterns most relevant to baseball (rotary stability, shoulder mobility)
- Monitor for decreases from baseline indicating fatigue or emerging issues
- Integrate corrective exercises into daily warmup routines

**Post-Injury Screening**:
- Use FMS/YBT as part of return-to-sport protocols
- Ensure bilateral symmetry restoration
- Verify movement quality matches or exceeds pre-injury baseline
- Progress gradually based on movement quality, not just time

### Training FMS/YBT Deficits in Baseball Players

**Deep Squat Limitations**:
- Ankle mobility: Calf stretches, ankle rockers
- Hip mobility: 90/90 hip stretches, Cossack squats
- Thoracic mobility: Foam roller extensions, quadruped rotations

**Hurdle Step Limitations**:
- Hip flexor length: Kneeling hip flexor stretches, dynamic leg swings
- Single-leg stability: Single-leg balance progressions, single-leg RDLs
- Hip strength: Monster walks, fire hydrants, clamshells

**Shoulder Mobility Limitations** (Critical for Baseball):
- Posterior shoulder: Sleeper stretches, cross-body stretches
- Lat length: Lat stretches, wall slides with reach
- Thoracic rotation: Open book exercises, side-lying rotations

**Rotary Stability Limitations** (Critical for Baseball):
- Anti-rotation: Pallof presses, single-arm farmer carries
- Diagonal patterns: Cable chops and lifts
- Bird dog progressions: Static holds → opposite arm/leg reaches → with resistance

## Key Research & Evidence

### Peer-Reviewed Studies

1. **FMS Comparison Between Preparative and Competitive Periods (2017)**: Study of high school baseball players found that while FMS scores differed between seasons, low sensitivity indicated FMS does not accurately predict injury risk during competitive periods. [Source: PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30662496/)

2. **Association of FMS and YBT with Injury (2019)**: Research on 124 high school athletes found FMS and YBT were not associated with increased risk of lower extremity injury, and findings did not support their use as stand-alone injury risk assessments. [Source: PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30844987/)

3. **Effects of FMS Training - Randomized Controlled Trial (2021)**: Study in PMC showed that after 12 weeks, good movement patterns were acquired, but improved FMS scores returned to original levels 12 weeks after ceasing training. [Source: PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8036127/)

4. **Efficacy of Injury Prevention Using FMS Training (2022)**: Research in PMC examining secondary outcomes of FMS training for injury prevention in high school baseball players. [Source: PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9777794/)

5. **Predictive Utility of FMS and YBT: Current Evidence (2025)**: Meta-analysis in PMC revealing that FMS and YBT overall scores often lack clinical relevance with significant variability in sensitivity and specificity. Multifactorial models considering workload ratios, injury history, and fitness data show better prediction. [Source: PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11860429/)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Using Screens as Pass/Fail Tests

Movement screens should identify training needs, not determine who can or cannot play. A low FMS score with no pain or injury history doesn't necessarily indicate injury risk.

### Ignoring Sport-Specific Context

Baseball players may score poorly on certain FMS movements (like deep squat) that aren't directly relevant to baseball demands, while excelling at sport-specific movements.

### One-Time Testing Without Follow-Up

Screens without subsequent training interventions and re-testing provide minimal value. The process is: screen → train → re-screen → adjust.

### Neglecting Other Risk Factors

Workload management, injury history, sleep quality, nutrition, and psychosocial factors often predict injury better than movement screens alone.

### Over-Correcting Asymmetries

Some asymmetries are normal and even adaptive in baseball (dominant-side differences). Not all asymmetries require correction.

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a physical therapist, athletic trainer, or sports medicine specialist if:

- Movement screens reveal pain during any pattern
- Significant asymmetries (>4 cm on YBT, bilateral FMS differences)
- FMS scores consistently ≤14 despite training interventions
- Declining movement quality despite consistent training
- History of recurrent injuries in areas where screens show deficits

Professional assessment can determine whether movement limitations stem from structural issues, previous injuries, motor control deficits, or training gaps—each requiring different interventions.
