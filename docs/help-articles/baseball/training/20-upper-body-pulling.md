# Upper Body Pulling: Rows, Pull-ups, and Face Pulls

## Quick Takeaways

- Pull-to-push ratio should be 2:1 for baseball athletes to maintain shoulder health
- Heavy rows strengthen lats, rhomboids, and mid-traps while stabilizing the shoulder joint
- Pull-ups and inverted rows build posterior chain strength essential for throwing deceleration
- Face pulls counteract forward shoulder posture from repetitive throwing
- Horizontal pulling should be emphasized over vertical pulling (2:1 ratio)

## Introduction

Upper body pulling exercises are non-negotiable for baseball athletes, particularly pitchers and position players who throw frequently. While pushing exercises often receive more attention in strength programs, research demonstrates that inadequate pulling volume creates muscular imbalances that lead to shoulder pathology, rounded posture, and decreased throwing performance.

Traditional weight lifting programs often demonstrate a strong focus on pressing exercises with less concern for pulling movements, creating a common muscular imbalance within the shoulder complex. For baseball athletes whose shoulders already experience tremendous anterior stress from throwing, emphasizing pulling work is essential for both performance and injury prevention.

## Research Supporting Pulling Exercises

### Push-Pull Balance

Research indicates that the push-to-pull ratio for the upper body should be approximately 1:2 for optimal shoulder health. The ideal ratio is 1.00 or higher, meaning equal or greater pulling strength compared to pushing strength. However, many athletes demonstrate ratios below 1.00, indicating anterior-dominant development that increases injury risk.

Muscular imbalances from overtraining one muscle group can lead to postural deterioration and shoulder pathology. Some research suggests this could cause rounded shoulder posture and conditions like subacromial impingement syndrome, which are common in overhead athletes.

### Injury Prevention

Research shows that greater injuries in baseball pitchers occur when they have greater internal rotator cuff strength and weaker external cuff muscles. Pulling exercises—particularly horizontal rowing and face pulls—strengthen the external rotators, posterior deltoid, and scapular retractors that balance the strong internal rotators developed through throwing.

## Horizontal Pulling Exercises

### Barbell and Dumbbell Rows

Heavy rows increase strength in the lats, rhomboids, and mid traps, while also strengthening stabilizing muscles around the shoulder joint and helping keep the humeral head centered within the glenoid fossa.

**Bent-Over Barbell Row:**

Technique:
- Hip hinge position (45-degree torso angle)
- Grip slightly wider than shoulder-width
- Pull bar to lower chest/upper abdomen
- Squeeze shoulder blades together at top
- Control descent

Programming:
- Load: 70-85% estimated 1RM
- Reps: 6-10
- Sets: 3-5
- Frequency: 2x per week

**Dumbbell Single-Arm Row:**

Technique:
- Hand and knee on bench, opposite foot on ground
- Neutral spine, slight hip hinge
- Pull dumbbell to hip
- Focus on scapular retraction
- Minimize trunk rotation

Programming:
- Load: 30-70 lbs per hand
- Reps: 8-12 per arm
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 2x per week

Baseball-Specific Benefits:
- Addresses side-to-side imbalances
- Develops lat strength for throwing deceleration
- Reinforces scapular control in athletic position

### Chest-Supported Rows

Eliminates lower back fatigue, allowing greater focus on upper back development—ideal for baseball athletes.

**Incline Bench Row:**

Setup:
- Incline bench set to 30-45 degrees
- Lie face down, feet on ground
- Dumbbells hang straight down

Execution:
- Pull dumbbells to sides of bench
- Retract scapulae fully
- Pause at top
- Control descent

Programming:
- Load: 25-50 lbs per hand
- Reps: 10-15
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 2x per week

Benefits:
- No lower back stress (important for pitchers)
- Pure upper back focus
- Can train to higher volumes safely

### Cable Rows

By varying cable row angles, athletes can pull from different positions, strengthening the rotator cuff by forcing it to stabilize the humeral head through various ranges of motion.

**Seated Cable Row:**

Programming:
- Load: 70-85% of pulling capacity
- Reps: 8-12
- Sets: 3-4
- Handle variations: Wide grip, narrow grip, single-arm

**Low-to-High Cable Row:**

Setup:
- Cable at lowest setting
- Stand in athletic stance
- Pull from low to high (diagonal pattern)

Benefits:
- Targets lower traps and rhomboids
- Mimics throwing follow-through deceleration
- Scapular upward rotation pattern

### Inverted Rows

Inverted rows and pullups are a great entry to muscle growth and building strength for the posterior chain, with bodyweight variations accessible to athletes of all levels.

**Setup:**
- Bar set at waist height (or use TRX/rings)
- Lie under bar, heels on ground
- Grip bar at shoulder width

**Execution:**
- Pull chest to bar
- Maintain straight body (plank position)
- Squeeze shoulder blades together
- Lower with control

**Programming:**
- Reps: 8-15
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 2-3x per week
- Progression: Elevate feet or add weight vest

## Vertical Pulling Exercises

For every vertical pulling variation performed weekly, you should perform twice the amount of horizontal pulling to optimize scapular health and function for baseball athletes.

### Pull-Ups and Chin-Ups

**Pull-Up (Pronated Grip):**

Technique:
- Hang from bar, hands slightly wider than shoulders
- Pull until chin over bar
- Control descent
- Full elbow extension at bottom

Programming:
- Reps: 5-10 (or max reps)
- Sets: 3-5
- Frequency: 2x per week
- Assistance: Use band or machine if needed

**Chin-Up (Supinated Grip):**

Benefits:
- Greater biceps involvement
- Often easier than pull-ups for beginners
- Excellent for developing arm strength

Programming:
- Reps: 6-12
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 1-2x per week

### Lat Pulldown

Regression or accessory work for pull-ups, allowing controlled loading.

**Programming:**
- Load: 60-80% bodyweight
- Reps: 10-15
- Sets: 3-4
- Frequency: 1-2x per week

**Grip Variations:**
- Wide grip: Greater lat emphasis
- Close neutral grip: More biceps and middle trap
- Single-arm: Address imbalances

## Face Pulls: The Essential Shoulder Health Exercise

Face pulls counteract the forward shoulder posture often developed from repetitive throwing motion and help balance the pushing strength developed in pressing exercises.

**Cable Face Pull Technique:**

Setup:
- Cable at face height
- Rope attachment
- Stand in athletic stance

Execution:
- Pull rope toward face
- Elbows high and wide
- Separate rope handles past ears
- Squeeze shoulder blades together
- 2-second hold at end range

Programming:
- Load: Light to moderate (15-30% of rowing weight)
- Reps: 15-20
- Sets: 3-5
- Frequency: 3-4x per week (can be done daily)

Baseball-Specific Benefits:
- Strengthens posterior deltoid and external rotators
- Improves scapular retraction and depression
- Reduces impingement risk
- Counteracts internal rotation dominance from throwing

## Comprehensive Pulling Program

### Off-Season Upper Body Day

**Monday - Heavy Horizontal Pulling:**
- Bent-over barbell row: 4x8 @ 80% 1RM
- Dumbbell single-arm row: 3x10 per arm
- Face pulls: 4x15-20
- Band pull-aparts: 3x25

**Thursday - Vertical Pulling + Accessory:**
- Pull-ups: 4 sets x max reps
- Chest-supported dumbbell row: 3x12
- Lat pulldown (close grip): 3x12
- Face pulls: 4x15-20
- YTW raises: 3x10 each position

### In-Season Maintenance

**2x Weekly:**
- Inverted row or cable row: 3x10-12
- Pull-ups or chin-ups: 3 sets x max reps
- Face pulls: 4x15-20
- Band external rotation: 3x15 per arm

### Pitcher-Specific (Non-Throwing Days)

**Primary Focus:**
- Cable row variation: 3x12
- Face pulls: 4x20
- Band pull-apart: 3x25
- YTW raises: 3x10 each

Volume: Lower intensity, higher frequency to maintain shoulder health without excessive fatigue.

## Pull-to-Push Volume Guidelines

**Horizontal Plane:**
- For every set of horizontal pressing (bench, push-ups)
- Perform 2 sets of horizontal pulling (rows)

**Vertical Plane:**
- For every set of vertical pressing (overhead press)
- Perform 2 sets of vertical pulling (pull-ups, lat pulldown)

**Weekly Volume Example:**
- Bench press: 12 total sets
- Rowing variations: 24+ total sets
- Overhead press: 6 total sets
- Pull-ups/pulldown: 12+ total sets
- Face pulls: 12-20 total sets (can be daily)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Using momentum instead of muscle:** Excessive body English during rows reduces effectiveness and increases injury risk. Use controlled tempo and feel the muscles working.

**Insufficient scapular retraction:** Simply moving the weight doesn't ensure proper scapular function. Actively squeeze shoulder blades together at end range of every pulling exercise.

**Neglecting face pulls:** Many programs include heavy rows and pull-ups but skip face pulls. This high-rep posterior deltoid and external rotator work is essential for shoulder health.

**Inadequate pulling volume:** If experiencing shoulder discomfort, first assessment should be pull-to-push ratio. Most baseball athletes need more pulling work, not less pushing.

**Training vertical pulling harder than horizontal:** Baseball demands primarily horizontal pulling strength for throwing deceleration. Emphasize rows over pull-ups.

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a sports medicine professional if you experience:
- Posterior shoulder pain during or after pulling exercises
- Scapular winging or inability to retract shoulder blades
- Elbow pain during rowing or pulling movements
- Significant strength asymmetry between sides (>15%)
- Chronic forward shoulder posture despite consistent pulling work
