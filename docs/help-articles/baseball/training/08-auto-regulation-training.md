# Auto-Regulation: Listening to Your Body

## Quick Takeaways
- Autoregulation adjusts training load based on daily readiness rather than fixed percentages
- RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) and RIR (Reps in Reserve) are primary autoregulation tools
- Research shows 12-18% greater strength gains with RPE-based protocols versus fixed-load programs
- Daily strength can fluctuate 15-20% based on sleep, stress, and recovery factors
- Autoregulation reduces overuse injuries by 34% while improving power output in collegiate athletes

## Introduction

Traditional strength programs prescribe fixed loads: "Squat 3×5 @ 85% 1RM on Monday." But what if you slept poorly, have an exam today, or played extra innings yesterday? Your 85% feels like 95%, and grinding through programmed weights may do more harm than good.

Autoregulation is a training methodology that adjusts weight, reps, and effort based on your daily performance and readiness. Rather than rigid percentages, autoregulation uses subjective measures (RPE, RIR) or objective measures (bar velocity, readiness scores) to modify training in real-time. For baseball players facing variable sleep, game schedules, and stress loads, autoregulation provides the flexibility to train optimally regardless of daily conditions.

## Understanding Autoregulation

**Autoregulation** means adjusting training variables based on current performance capacity rather than predetermined prescriptions. The core principle: your strength and work capacity fluctuate daily based on:
- Sleep quality and quantity
- Nutritional status
- Psychological stress (school, relationships, life)
- Accumulated training fatigue
- Game/practice demands
- Illness or minor injuries

Research confirms strength can fluctuate 15-20% depending on these factors. Fixed programming ignores this reality. Autoregulation acknowledges and adapts to it.

## Autoregulation Tools

### Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)
**Scale**: 1-10, where 10 = absolute maximum effort, 1 = minimal effort

**RPE Guidelines**:
- **RPE 10**: Maximum effort, no reps left in tank
- **RPE 9**: Could have done 1 more rep
- **RPE 8**: Could have done 2 more reps
- **RPE 7**: Could have done 3 more reps
- **RPE 6**: Could have done 4 more reps
- **RPE 5-6**: Moderate effort, "warm-up" feel

**Application**: "Squat 3×5 @ RPE 8" means perform 5 reps with a load that feels like you could do 2 more reps if forced.

### Reps in Reserve (RIR)
**Definition**: How many additional reps you could perform before failure

**RIR Guidelines**:
- **0 RIR**: Failure, no reps left
- **1 RIR**: Could do 1 more rep
- **2 RIR**: Could do 2 more reps
- **3 RIR**: Could do 3 more reps
- **4+ RIR**: Easy, multiple reps remaining

**Application**: "Bulgarian split squat 3×8 with 2 RIR" means stop each set when you could still do 2 more reps with good form.

RPE and RIR are inversely related:
- RPE 10 = 0 RIR
- RPE 9 = 1 RIR
- RPE 8 = 2 RIR
- RPE 7 = 3 RIR

### Bar Velocity
**Method**: Use velocity-based training (VBT) devices to measure bar speed
**Principle**: As fatigue accumulates, bar speed decreases even at same load
**Application**: "Continue sets until bar velocity drops 20% from first set"

**Example**:
- Set 1: 225 lbs at 0.75 m/s → continue
- Set 2: 225 lbs at 0.70 m/s → continue
- Set 3: 225 lbs at 0.60 m/s (20% drop) → end exercise

### Readiness Questionnaires
**Method**: Self-report surveys assessing sleep, soreness, stress, motivation
**Research**: A 2024 study found subjective readiness assessments detect training stress changes 38% faster than cortisol measurements

**Sample Questions** (1-10 scale):
- How well did you sleep last night?
- Rate your current muscle soreness
- Rate your current stress level
- Rate your motivation to train today
- How recovered do you feel?

**Application**: If readiness score <6/10, reduce volume by 20-30% or intensity by 5-10%

## The Science on Autoregulation

**2023-2024 Meta-Analysis**: Athletes using RPE-based protocols achieved 12-18% greater strength gains over 24 weeks compared to fixed-load programs. The ability to push harder on good days and back off on poor days optimized adaptation.

**2024 European Journal of Applied Physiology**: Combining velocity-based training with RPE reduced load prescription errors by 41% compared to percentage-based programming alone.

**University of Tokyo Study**: Evidence-based autoregulation reduced overuse injuries by 34% while improving power output by 9% in collegiate athletes over 12 weeks.

**Caveat - 2024 Mixed Results**: One 2024 study found autoregulation did not provide additional benefits to a periodized program in trained men when the extra volume was not beneficial. This suggests autoregulation's value lies in appropriate volume adjustment, not simply doing more work.

## Autoregulation Strategies for Baseball

### Daily Readiness Assessment
Before each training session, rate readiness 1-10:
- **8-10**: Train as programmed or increase intensity/volume slightly
- **6-7**: Train as programmed
- **4-5**: Reduce volume 20-30% OR reduce intensity 10%
- **1-3**: Active recovery only (movement, stretching, light throwing)

### RPE-Based Loading
Instead of "Trap bar deadlift 3×5 @ 85% 1RM":
- "Trap bar deadlift: Work up to 5RM @ RPE 9, then 2 back-off sets of 5 @ RPE 7-8"
- This accounts for daily fluctuation. On great days, you'll lift more. On poor days, less.

### In-Season Flexibility
**Traditional**: "Lift Tuesday and Friday regardless of schedule"
**Autoregulated**:
- Light game load this week → Lift Tuesday at RPE 8-9
- Heavy game load this week → Lift Tuesday at RPE 6-7
- Tournament weekend → Skip Friday session or RPE 5-6 maintenance

### Exercise Selection Autoregulation
Designate primary and alternative exercises:
- **If feeling great**: Trap bar deadlift (high CNS demand)
- **If fatigued**: Belt squat or leg press (lower CNS demand, similar muscle stimulus)
- **If minor soreness**: Machine variations (reduced stability requirement)

## Practical Implementation

**Start with RPE Calibration**
Week 1-2: After every set, record load/reps and estimate RPE. Then do an AMRAP (as many reps as possible) to see actual RIR. This calibrates your perception.

Example:
- Set of 5 at 185 lbs, felt like RPE 8 (2 RIR)
- Actual test: Got 7 reps total (only had 2 more reps) ✓ Accurate
- OR: Got 10 reps total (had 5 more reps) ✗ Recalibrate—that was RPE 6

**Use Hybrid Programming**
Combine percentage-based with autoregulation:
- "Squat: Work up to 80% for a top set @ RPE 9, then 3 sets of 5 @ 80% or RPE 7-8, whichever feels appropriate"
- This provides structure while allowing daily adjustment

**Document Everything**
Track prescribed RPE, actual RPE, loads used, and daily readiness:
- Date: 12/20/2024
- Readiness: 7/10 (okay sleep, low stress)
- Trap bar deadlift: 3×5 prescribed @ RPE 8
  - Set 1: 295 lbs × 5, RPE 8 ✓
  - Set 2: 295 lbs × 5, RPE 9 (fatigued faster than expected)
  - Set 3: 275 lbs × 5, RPE 8 (reduced load to hit target RPE)

**Develop Interoceptive Awareness**
Practice sensing fatigue, muscle tension, and effort. This skill improves with time and makes autoregulation more accurate.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Sandbagging**: Claiming every set is RPE 10 when it's actually RPE 7. This inflates ego but undermines training. Be honest.

**Overthinking**: Don't spend 10 minutes debating if a set was RPE 7.5 or 8. Make your best estimate and move on.

**No Boundaries**: Autoregulation isn't "do whatever you feel like." You still need structure. If prescribed 3 sets, do 3 sets. Adjust load/effort, not exercise selection or volume arbitrarily.

**Ignoring Objective Markers**: If your "RPE 8" set moves slow and looks like death, it's not RPE 8. Use video, bar speed, or coach feedback to reality-check perception.

**Autoregulating Recovery Sessions**: Easy days should stay easy (RPE 5-6). Don't autoregulate these up to RPE 8 because you "feel good." Recovery is programmed for a reason.

## When to Seek Professional Help

Work with a strength coach if:
- You struggle to accurately estimate RPE/RIR after 4+ weeks of practice
- You need help designing autoregulated programs for baseball-specific periodization
- You consistently feel fatigued despite adjusting load via autoregulation
- You want to integrate velocity-based training technology
- You're preparing for high-stakes showcases or professional events
