# Nerve Flossing for Baseball Players

## Overview

Nerve flossing—also called nerve gliding or neural mobilization—represents an emerging injury prevention strategy for baseball players, particularly pitchers. Recent research published in 2024 suggests that performing nerve flossing exercises before throwing may reduce the risk of ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) injury and other throwing-related pathologies. This article examines the science behind nerve flossing, presents evidence-based protocols specifically designed for baseball athletes, and explains how to integrate neural mobilization into your pre-throwing warm-up routine.

## The Science of Nerve Flossing

### Neural Mechanics in Throwing

During the throwing motion, extraordinary neural stress occurs throughout the arm. Research on throwing biomechanics reveals that an average of 12.4 millimeters of nerve movement occurs at the elbow and 8.2 millimeters at the wrist during each pitch. Strain within the ulnar nerve proximal to the cubital tunnel measures 13.1%—approaching the elastic and circulatory limits of the nerve.

These extreme forces create what researchers call "adverse neural tension"—a condition where nerves become stiff or restricted in their movement capacity. Studies indicate that nerve stiffness may contribute to compensatory movement patterns that increase stress on passive stabilizers like the UCL.

### The Nerve Flossing Mechanism

A groundbreaking 2024 clinical suggestion published in the *International Journal of Sports Physical Therapy* proposes that nerve flossing techniques may be an effective avenue to combat UCL injury among baseball pitchers. The proposed holistic pretreatment approach utilizing radial, median, and ulnar nerve glides prior to competition may proactively impede development of adverse neural tension.

Nerve flossing works by gently mobilizing nerves through their anatomical pathways, reducing stiffness and improving nerve excursion (ability to slide and glide). While research to date has focused primarily on lower limbs and rehabilitation contexts, the biomechanical rationale for upper extremity application in throwing athletes is compelling.

### Research Limitations and Promise

Researchers acknowledge that while nerve flossing has been found useful in rehabilitation or in cases of documented neural tension, high-quality studies specifically examining preventive applications in baseball are limited. However, the extreme neural stress documented in pitching biomechanics provides strong theoretical support for prophylactic nerve mobilization.

Though research indicates the effects of nerve glides are temporary in their performance enhancement capability, performing nerve flossing of the radial, median, and ulnar nerves prior to maximum-effort throwing may be a practical strategy to support the integrity of neural and connective tissues, potentially reducing the risk of ligamentous strain or injury.

## Three-Nerve Protocol for Baseball

### Pre-Throwing Nerve Flossing Sequence (6-8 Minutes)

Complete this protocol after general dynamic warm-up but before throwing or resistance band work.

**Timing:** Between general warm-up and arm care in your routine

**Frequency:** Before every throwing session for pitchers; 2-3x weekly for position players

## Ulnar Nerve Flossing

**Anatomy:** The ulnar nerve runs from the neck, down the inside of the arm, through the cubital tunnel at the elbow (funny bone area), and into the hand (ring and pinky fingers).

**Baseball Relevance:** Highest stress during throwing; ulnar nerve subluxation is common among professional pitchers.

### Ulnar Nerve Glide Technique

**Starting Position:**
- Stand or sit comfortably
- Arm at side, shoulder relaxed
- Palm facing forward

**Movement Sequence (3 sets of 15 repetitions):**

1. **Extension Phase:**
   - Extend elbow fully
   - Wrist extended (hand back)
   - Fingers spread
   - Gently laterally flex neck AWAY from gliding arm
   - Hold 2 seconds

2. **Flexion Phase:**
   - Flex elbow (bring hand toward shoulder)
   - Wrist flexed (hand forward)
   - Fingers together
   - Return neck to neutral
   - Hold 2 seconds

**Key Points:**
- Smooth, controlled movement
- Should feel gentle pulling/tension, NOT pain
- If sharp pain or tingling occurs, reduce range of motion
- Alternate with passive range of motion (PROM) between sets

**Duration:** 2-3 minutes total

## Median Nerve Flossing

**Anatomy:** The median nerve travels down the arm through the carpal tunnel at the wrist, innervating the thumb, index, middle, and half of the ring finger.

**Baseball Relevance:** Important for grip strength and fine motor control of pitches.

### Median Nerve Glide Technique

**Starting Position:**
- Stand with arm at side
- Palm facing backward (pronated)
- Fingers relaxed

**Movement Sequence (3 sets of 15 repetitions):**

1. **Extension Phase:**
   - Extend arm out to side at shoulder height
   - External rotation (palm faces up/forward)
   - Wrist extended (hand back)
   - Fingers spread
   - Gently tilt head AWAY from arm
   - Hold 2 seconds

2. **Flexion Phase:**
   - Bring arm back to side
   - Internal rotation (palm faces back)
   - Wrist relaxed to neutral
   - Return head to neutral
   - Hold 2 seconds

**Key Points:**
- Gentle tension through forearm and palm
- Stop if numbness or tingling in fingers
- Can perform seated or standing
- Smooth, rhythmic movement

**Duration:** 2-3 minutes total

## Radial Nerve Flossing

**Anatomy:** The radial nerve runs down the back of the arm, wrapping around the humerus, and innervates the back of the hand and thumb extension.

**Baseball Relevance:** Important for wrist extension and grip release in pitching.

### Radial Nerve Glide Technique

**Starting Position:**
- Stand comfortably
- Arm at side
- Palm facing thigh

**Movement Sequence (3 sets of 15 repetitions):**

1. **Extension Phase:**
   - Internally rotate shoulder (thumb points behind you)
   - Extend elbow
   - Flex wrist (hand downward)
   - Fingers curled
   - Laterally flex neck AWAY from arm
   - Hold 2 seconds

2. **Flexion Phase:**
   - Rotate shoulder back to neutral
   - Slight elbow flexion
   - Wrist returns to neutral
   - Return head to neutral
   - Hold 2 seconds

**Key Points:**
- Should feel tension along back of arm
- Gentler than ulnar and median (radial nerve more superficial)
- Stop if sharp pain in elbow or wrist
- Very controlled movement

**Duration:** 2-3 minutes total

## Recommended Protocol for Pitchers

Research suggests an example warm-up protocol involving:

**Three sets of 15 practitioner-assisted repetitions of radial, median, and ulnar nerve flossing, with each set alternated by passive range of motion (PROM) mobilization or contract-relax stretching techniques such as proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation (PNF) or muscle energy technique (MET).**

### Practical Implementation (Solo or Partner-Assisted)

**Solo Protocol (6-8 minutes):**
1. Ulnar nerve glides: 3 sets of 15 reps (2 min)
2. Shoulder PROM or arm circles (1 min)
3. Median nerve glides: 3 sets of 15 reps (2 min)
4. Shoulder PROM or PNF stretch (1 min)
5. Radial nerve glides: 3 sets of 15 reps (2 min)
6. Final PROM mobilization (1 min)

**Partner-Assisted Protocol (8-10 minutes):**
- Trainer or partner assists with positioning
- Allows more precise technique
- Can apply gentle contract-relax between sets
- Optimal for pitchers with documented neural tension

## Integration with Overall Warm-up

### Complete Pre-Throwing Sequence

**Optimal Order:**
1. General dynamic warm-up (10 minutes)
2. **Nerve flossing protocol (6-8 minutes)** ← Insert here
3. Scapular and rotator cuff activation (10 minutes)
4. Resistance band work (5 minutes)
5. Progressive throwing (10 minutes)

**Rationale:** Nerve flossing after general warm-up ensures tissues are warm, but performing before resistance work and throwing allows neural mobility to support subsequent movements.

## Position-Specific Recommendations

### Starting Pitchers

**Frequency:** Daily during season
**Emphasis:** All three nerves, full protocol
**Integration:** Non-negotiable part of pre-game and bullpen warm-ups

### Relief Pitchers

**Frequency:** Daily during season
**Emphasis:** Ulnar nerve priority, abbreviated protocol acceptable
**Integration:** Pre-game foundation work

### Position Players

**Frequency:** 2-3 times weekly
**Emphasis:** Ulnar and median nerves
**Integration:** Throwing days and heavy training days

### Catchers

**Frequency:** 4-5 times weekly (high throwing volume from crouch)
**Emphasis:** All three nerves
**Integration:** Before all throwing sessions

## Identifying Neural Tension

### Signs You May Benefit from Nerve Flossing

**Symptoms Suggesting Adverse Neural Tension:**
- Persistent tightness in forearm that doesn't respond to stretching
- Intermittent numbness or tingling in fingers
- "Dead arm" sensation
- Elbow pain without clear structural cause
- Reduced grip strength
- Symptoms that worsen with throwing volume

**Important:** If you experience these symptoms, consult a sports medicine professional. Nerve flossing is a preventive and adjunctive technique, not a replacement for proper diagnosis and treatment.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Excessive Force or Speed

Nerve flossing should be gentle and controlled. Aggressive, rapid movements defeat the purpose and may irritate neural tissue. Think "mobilization" not "stretching."

### Pushing Through Pain

Sharp pain, electric sensations, or increasing numbness indicate excessive neural stress. Reduce range of motion immediately. You should feel gentle tension, not pain.

### Inconsistent Application

Research suggests benefits are temporary. Performing nerve flossing once weekly won't provide protective effects. Daily application for pitchers during season is recommended.

### Skipping After Symptoms Resolve

If nerve flossing helps resolve minor neural symptoms, continue as preventive maintenance. Don't stop when symptoms disappear.

## Evidence Level and Recommendations

### Current Research Status

The 2024 clinical suggestion represents novel, theoretically-sound guidance based on:
- Documented extreme neural stress in pitching
- Established nerve flossing benefits in other contexts
- Biomechanical rationale for preventive application

However, researchers note: "Much of the limited research to date has focused on the lower limbs" and call for high-quality studies specifically examining upper extremity applications in throwing athletes.

### Practical Recommendation

Given the:
- Low risk of properly-performed nerve flossing
- Extreme documented neural stress in pitching
- Theoretical mechanism supporting preventive benefit
- Emerging clinical support

The risk-to-benefit ratio favors incorporating nerve flossing into pre-throwing routines, particularly for pitchers and high-volume throwers.

## Age-Specific Guidelines

### Youth (Ages 8-12)
- Generally not necessary unless specific symptoms present
- If implemented: 1 set of 10 reps per nerve
- Teach proper technique with supervision

### Adolescent (Ages 13-18)
- Begin incorporating for pitchers
- 2 sets of 12 reps per nerve
- Particularly important during growth spurts
- Professional instruction recommended

### College/Professional
- Full protocol as described
- May benefit from practitioner-assisted technique
- Consider adding to recovery protocols

## Monitoring and Adjustment

**Track These Indicators:**
- Forearm tightness levels
- Any numbness or tingling occurrence
- Grip strength consistency
- Throwing comfort and command

**Positive Response Signs:**
- Reduced forearm tightness
- Better arm "feel" when throwing
- Maintained velocity through outings
- No neural symptoms

**Adjust if:**
- Symptoms worsen (consult medical professional)
- No perceived benefit after 2-3 weeks (technique evaluation needed)
- Time constraints (prioritize ulnar nerve)

## Key Takeaways

Nerve flossing represents an emerging injury prevention strategy with promising theoretical support for baseball players. Research published in 2024 suggests that radial, median, and ulnar nerve mobilization before throwing may reduce adverse neural tension and potentially prevent UCL and other throwing-related injuries. During pitching, nerves experience extreme stress—12.4mm of movement at the elbow and 13.1% strain approaching physiological limits. A 6-8 minute protocol performing 3 sets of 15 repetitions for each nerve, integrated between general warm-up and resistance band work, provides practical preventive intervention. While high-quality research specifically validating this approach in baseball is still emerging, the low risk of properly-performed nerve flossing combined with documented extreme neural stress in throwing supports incorporating this technique, particularly for pitchers. Proper technique emphasizes gentle, controlled movements—never forcing into pain or sharp sensations. This represents a novel, evidence-informed addition to comprehensive arm care programs.
