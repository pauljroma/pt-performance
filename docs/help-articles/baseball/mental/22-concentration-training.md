# Concentration Training: Staying Locked In

## Quick Takeaways

- Concentration is the ability to focus on relevant cues while ignoring distractions
- Attention has four dimensions: broad/narrow and internal/external, requiring flexibility in baseball
- Research shows concentration is trainable through specific exercises and deliberate practice
- Baseball's stop-start nature creates unique concentration challenges requiring constant refocusing
- Elite players develop systematic concentration routines for different game situations

## Introduction

A fastball reaches home plate in 0.4 seconds. In that tiny window, a hitter must identify pitch type, location, and velocity, then decide whether to swing and execute that decision—all while blocking out crowd noise, game situations, batting average thoughts, and a thousand other potential distractions.

Concentration—the ability to focus on relevant task cues while ignoring irrelevant distractions—might be baseball's most critical mental skill. Unlike continuous sports where concentration can flow for extended periods, baseball requires constant refocusing: between pitches, between at-bats, between innings, across nine innings and 162-game seasons.

The encouraging news: concentration is trainable. Research reports improvements in baseball players after performance enhancement training including focused attention exercises. Peak performance occurs when athletes are able to voluntarily concentrate on the cues in their environment and perceive them to demand an action that is within their ability to execute.

## Understanding Attention Dimensions

Attention isn't one-dimensional. Research identifies four types of attentional focus:

### Broad-External

Attending to multiple environmental cues simultaneously:
- **Baseball Example**: Pitcher reading multiple runners on base, fielder positioning, batter stance
- **When Needed**: Situation assessment, defensive positioning, pre-pitch read

### Narrow-External

Focusing on a single external cue:
- **Baseball Example**: Hitter focusing only on ball out of pitcher's hand, pitcher focusing on catcher's target
- **When Needed**: Pitch execution, hitting, fielding a batted ball

### Broad-Internal

Attending to multiple internal thoughts or strategies:
- **Baseball Example**: Analyzing pitcher's pattern, reviewing scouting report, developing game plan
- **When Needed**: Between innings, in dugout, pre-game preparation

### Narrow-Internal

Focusing on single internal cue like a feeling or thought:
- **Baseball Example**: Focusing on breathing, monitoring arousal level, sensing muscle tension
- **When Needed**: Arousal regulation, pre-performance centering, recovery from mistakes

**Key Insight**: Elite players shift flexibly between these attention styles depending on situation demands. Poor concentration often results from using the wrong attentional focus for the situation.

## Concentration Challenges in Baseball

### Stop-Start Nature

Unlike continuous sports, baseball requires constant refocusing:
- After each pitch (every 20-30 seconds)
- Between at-bats (every 3-5 minutes)
- Between innings (every 15-20 minutes)
- Across long games (3+ hours)

Each transition is an opportunity to lose focus if you don't have systematic refocusing strategies.

### High Distraction Environment

**External Distractions**:
- Crowd noise and movement
- Opposing team chatter
- Weather conditions
- Umpire inconsistency
- Media presence

**Internal Distractions**:
- Thoughts about performance ("I'm 0-3")
- Situational pressure ("We need this run")
- Physical discomfort (fatigue, minor aches)
- Future concerns ("Next inning I'm leading off")
- Past mistakes ("I should have thrown changeup")

### Boring Periods

Fielders may go innings without a ball hit their direction. Maintaining readiness during inactivity challenges concentration.

## Training Concentration Skills

### Exercise 1: Controllables vs. Uncontrollables

Make two lists:
- **Controllables**: Factors you can directly influence (your effort, preparation, approach, routine, self-talk)
- **Uncontrollables**: Factors outside your control (umpire calls, opponent quality, weather, teammates' performance)

Practice shifting attention to controllables whenever you notice focus drifting to uncontrollables. This exercise trains selective attention.

### Exercise 2: Focused Object Meditation

**Practice** (5-10 minutes daily):
1. Choose an object (baseball, glove, photograph)
2. Focus all attention on it—notice details, textures, colors
3. When mind wanders, acknowledge and return focus to object
4. Repeat hundreds of times

This trains the fundamental concentration skill: noticing when attention drifts and redirecting it.

### Exercise 3: Distraction Resilience Training

Simulation training places athletes in "real life" scenarios during practice to simulate possible distractions and additional areas of focus:

**In Practice**:
- Have teammates create distractions during bullpen or batting practice
- Practice with loud music or noise
- Add time pressure artificially
- Create high-stakes consequences for performance

Learning to maintain focus despite distractions transfers to games.

### Exercise 4: Attention Switching Drills

Practice deliberately shifting between attention dimensions:

**Drill Example for Hitters**:
1. Broad-Internal: Review pitcher's tendencies (20 seconds)
2. Narrow-Internal: Center with breath focus (5 seconds)
3. Broad-External: Read fielder positioning (5 seconds)
4. Narrow-External: Lock on to pitcher's release point (100% focus)

This builds flexibility in attentional control.

### Exercise 5: Mindfulness Practice

Research indicates that mindfulness practices increase athletes' ability to enhance concentration so that attention remains on the task at hand during action if desired.

**Basic Practice** (10 minutes daily):
- Sit quietly, focus on breathing
- Notice when mind wanders
- Gently return attention to breath
- Repeat this cycle hundreds of times

Each "notice-and-return" strengthens concentration like a mental rep.

## Position-Specific Concentration Strategies

### Pitchers: Between-Pitch Focus

**After Previous Pitch** (5-10 seconds):
- Receive ball from catcher
- Broad-internal: Quick assessment of what happened, what to adjust
- Narrow-internal: Centering breath
- Release any frustration or excitement

**Before Next Pitch** (5-10 seconds):
- Broad-external: Check runners, fielders
- Narrow-external: Lock onto target
- Narrow-internal: Commit to pitch selection
- Narrow-external: Focus entirely on target as you begin motion

### Hitters: Between-Pitch Focus

**After Previous Pitch** (10-15 seconds):
- Step out of box (physical separation)
- Broad-internal: What did I learn? What's next?
- Narrow-internal: Centering breath, reset arousal
- Broad-external: See pitcher's delivery
- Narrow-external: Lock on to release point

**During Pitch** (0.4 seconds):
- Narrow-external ONLY: See the ball
- No thinking, pure reaction

### Fielders: Maintaining Readiness

**Before Each Pitch**:
- Broad-internal: Situation check (outs, runners, plays)
- Broad-external: Position adjustment
- Narrow-external: Read pitcher-hitter contact point
- Physical activation (small hop as pitch is thrown)

**During Boring Periods**:
- Use each pitch as a concentration opportunity
- Self-talk: "Next one could be to me"
- Visualization of making plays
- Physical readiness stance maintains focus

## Developing Pre-Performance Concentration Routines

Pre-performance routines help athletes block out irrelevant internal and external distractions by giving them something to focus on.

**Elements of Concentration Routines**:

1. **Physical Trigger**: A specific action that initiates focus (stepping into box, touching cap, positioning on rubber)

2. **Attentional Narrowing**: Deliberately shift from broad to narrow focus

3. **External Focus**: Direct attention to relevant environmental cue (ball, target, release point)

4. **Execution**: Perform with quiet mind, trusting preparation

**Example Pitcher Routine**:
- Receive ball (physical trigger)
- Look at target (broad-external to narrow-external)
- Deep breath (narrow-internal for centering)
- Final look at target (locked narrow-external)
- Execute (quiet mind)

## Common Concentration Errors

**Broad When Need Narrow**: Thinking about multiple things during pitch execution. Analysis belongs between pitches, not during them.

**Internal When Need External**: Focusing on body mechanics or thoughts during performance instead of external task cues.

**Past/Future Focus**: Dwelling on previous mistakes or worrying about upcoming situations instead of staying present.

**Forcing Concentration**: Trying too hard to focus creates tension and overthinking. Effective concentration is relaxed awareness.

**No Refocusing System**: Lacking systematic strategies for regaining focus after distractions or mistakes.

**Multitasking**: Trying to focus on everything simultaneously. Effective concentration involves selective attention—focusing on the right thing at the right time.

## Measuring Concentration Improvement

Track these indicators:

**Error Recovery**: Speed of refocusing after mistakes decreases
**Distraction Immunity**: External distractions (noise, chatter) have less impact
**Routine Consistency**: Pre-performance routines executed without thinking
**Present-Moment Absorption**: More frequent experiences of being "locked in"
**Between-Play Quality**: Effective use of time between pitches/at-bats for refocusing

## Integration with Other Mental Skills

**Mindfulness**: Provides foundation for concentration—awareness of where attention is directed

**Imagery**: Visualization can be a concentration focus between performances

**Self-Talk**: Directs attention to relevant cues ("see ball deep," "attack target")

**Pre-Performance Routines**: Systematize the shift to optimal attentional focus

## Advanced Concentration Technique: Attention Training Techniques (ATT)

Research on a 12-week ATT training program found that training can influence athletes' experiences of being able to switch from a mind-wandering state to take executive control of their attention.

**ATT Principles**:
- Practice sustained focus on chosen stimuli
- Practice switching attention deliberately
- Practice divided attention (multiple relevant cues)
- Practice recognizing and disengaging from irrelevant stimuli

These skills directly transfer to baseball's varied attention demands.
