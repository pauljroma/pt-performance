# Mobility Screening: Identifying Your Limitations

## Overview

Systematic mobility screening represents the foundation for developing individualized training programs that address specific limitations rather than applying generic approaches. Research demonstrates that movement screens can identify athletes at higher risk for injury and reveal mobility deficits that compromise performance. This article explores evidence-based screening methods, interpretation guidelines, and program development based on individual assessment results.

## The Science of Movement Screening

### Functional Movement Screen (FMS) in Baseball

**Overview:** The FMS is a screening tool, not intended for diagnosis, that aims to identify imbalances in mobility and stability during functional movements. The FMS consists of seven movement patterns which require mobility and stability, with each scored from 0-3 points, creating a total score ranging from 0-21 points.

**Use in Baseball:** Baseball and softball athletes have been screened using the FMS at various training facilities. Research has evaluated its effectiveness for identifying injury risk and performance limitations specific to baseball players.

### Research Findings in Baseball Players

**Injury Prediction:** A study comparing FMS scores revealed significantly higher values for shoulder mobility on both sides and lower values for rotary stability on the dominant side in injured versus non-injured high school baseball pitchers.

**Upper Extremity Screening:** The FMS shoulder mobility and SFMA (Selective Functional Movement Assessment) upper extremity patterns may provide value in pre-season screening for collegiate baseball players, as these screens can identify individuals who have limited or painful mobility.

**Overuse Symptoms:** Research examining the relationship of preseason movement screens with overuse symptoms in collegiate baseball players found connections between specific movement limitations and injury occurrence.

### Important Limitations of Screening

**Predictive Validity:** Research to date is limited, and it is not yet clear for which sports or professions the FMS is optimal in predicting injury risk, and whether the original cutoff score of ≤14 is valid across all populations.

**Total Score Limitations:** The use of a total FMS score alone for predicting injury risk may not be sufficient, as performance assessments executed at sports-specific demands and speeds differ substantially from fundamental movements. Power, endurance, change of direction, and other functional constructs are not included in the FMS screening.

**Controversial Evidence:** Whether the FMS can be used as a tool to predict injuries remains controversial, with some studies showing predictive ability for identifying athletes at higher risk of injury, but others proposing no predictive capabilities based on low sensitivity and low AUC (area under curve).

**Influencing Factors:** Factors may impact scoring on movement screens including fatigue, rater training, reliability, and variability of verbal cues provided to athletes, with some of these factors studied extensively while other areas lack evidence.

## Comprehensive Baseball Mobility Screening Protocol

### Pre-Screening Considerations

**Timing:**
- Pre-season (optimal baseline)
- Post-injury (return-to-play assessment)
- Mid-season (monitoring)
- Monthly (tracking progress)

**Environment:**
- Consistent location
- Adequate space
- Proper lighting for video
- No fatigue (not immediately post-practice)

**Documentation:**
- Video all tests
- Record measurements precisely
- Note pain or discomfort
- Compare to previous assessments

### Upper Body Screening Tests

#### 1. Shoulder Mobility Screen

**Overhead Reach Test:**
- Stand with arms at sides
- Simultaneously reach one hand overhead/down back, other hand up back
- Attempt to touch hands or measure distance apart
- Perform both sides

**Scoring:**
- Hands touch: 3 points
- Within one fist width: 2 points
- Greater than one fist width: 1 point
- Pain during test: 0 points

**Baseball-Specific Considerations:**
- Throwing arm often more limited in this pattern
- Asymmetry >1.5 inches warrants attention
- Pain requires professional evaluation

#### 2. Shoulder Internal/External Rotation

**Supine 90/90 Test:**
- Lie on back, shoulder at 90° abduction
- Elbow bent 90°
- Measure internal rotation (hand toward feet)
- Measure external rotation (hand toward head)

**Norms for Throwers:**
- External rotation: 110-130° (throwing arm)
- Internal rotation: 50-70° (throwing arm)
- Total arc: 170-180° both arms
- ER gain/IR deficit: Common adaptation

**Red Flags:**
- Total arc motion difference >5° between arms
- Internal rotation deficit >20° compared to non-throwing arm
- Pain at end ranges

#### 3. Thoracic Rotation Screen

**Lumbar Locked Rotation Test:**
- Sit on bench, feet flat
- Cross arms over chest
- Partner stabilizes pelvis
- Rotate maximally to each side

**Scoring:**
- 50-60° each direction: Normal
- 40-49°: Moderate limitation
- <40°: Significant limitation
- Asymmetry >10°: Address imbalance

#### 4. Scapular Dyskinesis Test

**Procedure:**
- Stand with arms at sides
- Perform 5 repetitions of arm raises (weighted 3-5 lbs)
- Observer watches from behind
- Note scapular positioning and movement

**Scoring:**
- Normal: Symmetric, smooth movement
- Subtle dyskinesis: Slight asymmetry or dysrhythmia
- Obvious dyskinesis: Clear winging, tilting, or dysrhythmia

**Common Patterns:**
- Inferior angle prominence (lower trap weakness)
- Medial border prominence (serratus weakness)
- Early/excessive elevation (upper trap dominance)

### Lower Body Screening Tests

#### 5. Deep Squat Assessment

**Procedure:**
- Feet shoulder-width apart
- Dowel held overhead
- Squat as deep as possible
- Maintain dowel overhead, heels down

**Scoring:**
- Torso parallel to tibia, dowel aligned: 3 points
- Achieves depth with board under heels: 2 points
- Cannot achieve depth or loses balance: 1 point
- Pain: 0 points

**Reveals:**
- Ankle dorsiflexion limitations
- Hip mobility restrictions
- Thoracic extension capacity
- Overall mobility/stability balance

#### 6. Hip Mobility Assessment

**A. Hip Internal Rotation (Seated):**
- Sit with knees bent 90°
- Rotate lower leg outward (measures IR)
- Measure angle from vertical

**Norms:**
- 40-45° both hips
- Symmetry within 5°

**B. Hip External Rotation (Seated):**
- Same position
- Rotate lower leg inward (measures ER)
- Measure angle from vertical

**Norms:**
- 45-50° both hips
- Pitchers: Back hip often more ER

**C. Hip Flexion (Supine):**
- Pull knee to chest
- Measure angle between thigh and trunk

**Norms:**
- 120-130° minimum
- No lumbar flexion compensation

**D. Hip Extension (Modified Thomas Test):**
- Lie on table edge
- Pull one knee to chest
- Opposite leg hangs off edge

**Norms:**
- 10-15° hip extension
- No anterior pelvic tilt

#### 7. Ankle Dorsiflexion Screen

**Weight-Bearing Lunge Test:**
- Face wall in half-kneeling
- Front foot 5 inches from wall
- Drive knee toward wall
- Gradually increase distance

**Scoring:**
- 5+ inches: Excellent (3 points)
- 3-4.9 inches: Adequate (2 points)
- <3 inches: Limited (1 point)
- Pain: 0 points

**Minimum Standard:**
- 4-5 inches for baseball athletes
- Symmetry within 1 inch

#### 8. Hamstring Flexibility

**Active Straight Leg Raise:**
- Supine position
- Actively raise one leg
- Keep opposite leg flat
- Maintain neutral pelvis

**Norms:**
- 70-80° active raise
- 80-90° passive raise
- Difference indicates motor control vs. tissue restriction

### Rotational Screening Tests

#### 9. Rotary Stability Screen

**Procedure:**
- Quadruped position
- Lift same-side arm and leg
- Touch elbow to knee
- Alternate pattern if unable

**Scoring:**
- Correct pattern without compensation: 3 points
- Alternate pattern (opposite arm/leg): 2 points
- Cannot perform either: 1 point
- Pain: 0 points

**Baseball Relevance:**
- Throwing arm side often more challenged
- Indicates core rotational stability
- Important for injury prevention

#### 10. Trunk Rotation Screen

**Seated Rotation with Dowel:**
- Sit on bench, dowel across shoulders
- Partner stabilizes knees
- Rotate maximally to each side

**Norms:**
- 50-60° each direction
- Asymmetry <10°

**Baseball-Specific:**
- May see asymmetry toward throwing side
- Excessive limitation indicates mobility work needed

## Interpreting Screening Results

### Identifying Priority Areas

**High Priority (Address Immediately):**
- Any test scored 0 (pain present)
- Asymmetries >15° in rotation tests
- Failed fundamental movements (deep squat)
- Total motion deficits in throwing shoulder

**Moderate Priority (Address Systematically):**
- Asymmetries 10-15°
- Scores of 1 on multiple tests
- Unilateral limitations
- Moderate restrictions in key areas

**Low Priority (Monitor and Maintain):**
- Minor asymmetries (<10°)
- Scores of 2 on most tests
- Areas meeting minimum standards

### Pattern Recognition

**Common Baseball Patterns:**

**Throwing Arm Adaptations:**
- Increased external rotation
- Decreased internal rotation
- Total arc motion within normal
- Not pathological if no pain

**Pitcher-Specific Limitations:**
- Reduced hip internal rotation (lead leg)
- Thoracic rotation asymmetry
- Scapular dyskinesis (throwing side)

**Position Player Patterns:**
- Bilateral hip limitations from squatting/running
- Symmetrical deficits more common
- Lower extremity emphasis

### Red Flags Requiring Professional Evaluation

**Immediate Referral:**
- Pain during any screening test
- Significant asymmetries (>20°) in key areas
- Inability to perform fundamental movements
- Neurological symptoms (numbness, weakness)
- Recent injury or pain

**Consider Referral:**
- No improvement after 4-6 weeks of intervention
- Multiple limitations across systems
- Functional performance not matching screen results
- Athlete or parent concern

## Developing Individualized Mobility Programs

### Step 1: Categorize Findings

**Mobility Deficits:**
- List all areas with limited ROM
- Prioritize by impact on performance
- Note pain-free vs. painful limitations

**Stability Deficits:**
- Identify areas with poor motor control
- Note compensation patterns
- Consider strength vs. coordination issues

**Asymmetries:**
- Document side-to-side differences
- Determine if adaptive or problematic
- Plan for addressing imbalances

### Step 2: Create Targeted Intervention Plan

**For Mobility Limitations:**
- Daily stretching: 30-60 seconds, 3 sets
- Foam rolling: 60-90 seconds affected areas
- Movement-based mobility work
- Progress based on reassessment

**For Stability Issues:**
- Activation exercises: 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets
- Progressive loading patterns
- Motor control drills
- Integration with sport movements

**For Asymmetries:**
- Additional work on limited side (1.5-2x volume)
- Unilateral exercises
- Mirror work for awareness
- Monitor for overcorrection

### Step 3: Program Template

**Daily Routine (15-20 minutes):**
1. Foam rolling priority areas: 5-7 minutes
2. Targeted stretching (top 3-4 limitations): 8-10 minutes
3. Activation exercises (stability deficits): 5 minutes

**3x Weekly Extended Session (30-35 minutes):**
1. Comprehensive foam rolling: 10 minutes
2. Full mobility routine: 15 minutes
3. Stability and strength work: 10 minutes

**Integration with Baseball Activities:**
- Pre-throwing: Abbreviated routine
- Post-throwing: Recovery-focused work
- Off-days: Extended development sessions

### Step 4: Reassessment Schedule

**Weekly:**
- Subjective feel (1-10 scale for each area)
- Quick screen of priority areas
- Adjust program based on progress

**Monthly:**
- Full screening protocol
- Document measurements
- Update priorities and program
- Video for comparison

**Quarterly:**
- Comprehensive assessment
- Update baseline measurements
- Long-term planning adjustments

## Position-Specific Screening Protocols

### Pitchers

**Emphasis Areas:**
1. Shoulder total arc of motion
2. Scapular function
3. Hip mobility (both internal/external rotation)
4. Thoracic rotation
5. Ankle dorsiflexion

**Critical Thresholds:**
- Shoulder TAM difference <5°
- Hip ER ≥45° (back leg)
- Thoracic rotation ≥45° each side
- Scapular dyskinesis: Subtle or less

### Position Players

**Emphasis Areas:**
1. Lower extremity symmetry
2. Hip mobility (bilateral)
3. Ankle mobility
4. Shoulder function (throwing arm)
5. Core rotational stability

**Critical Thresholds:**
- Hip asymmetry <10° (IR/ER)
- Ankle dorsiflexion ≥4 inches
- Deep squat score ≥2
- Trunk rotation ≥50° each side

### Catchers

**Additional Screening:**
- Deeper squat assessment (occupational demand)
- Hip flexion and internal rotation (critical)
- Ankle dorsiflexion (both feet)
- Thoracic extension capacity

## Conclusion

Systematic mobility screening provides the foundation for evidence-based, individualized program development in baseball players. While the predictive validity of screening tools like the FMS continues to be researched and debated, these assessments offer valuable information about an athlete's current mobility status, asymmetries, and potential limitations that may impact performance or increase injury risk.

The key to effective screening lies not in achieving a specific total score, but in identifying individual movement limitations, understanding baseball-specific patterns (such as throwing arm adaptations), and developing targeted interventions to address priority areas. Research supports the value of shoulder mobility and upper extremity screening in baseball players, while also highlighting the importance of lower body mobility for optimal performance.

By conducting comprehensive screening pre-season, monitoring throughout the competitive year, and developing individualized programs based on findings, baseball players and their coaches can make informed decisions about training priorities. The combination of systematic assessment, targeted intervention, and regular reassessment creates a framework for optimizing mobility, addressing limitations before they become injuries, and supporting long-term athletic development.

Remember: Screening is the beginning of the process, not the end. The value comes from acting on the information gathered and tracking progress over time.
