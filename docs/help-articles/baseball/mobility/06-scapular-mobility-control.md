# Scapular Mobility and Control Exercises

## Overview

Scapular dysfunction represents one of the most prevalent issues in baseball throwers, with dyskinesis identified in 50-100% of throwers with shoulder or arm injuries. Research demonstrates that scapular mobility and control are foundational to shoulder health, throwing performance, and injury prevention. This article explores evidence-based assessment methods and progressive exercises to optimize scapular function in baseball players.

## The Science of Scapular Function in Throwing

### Prevalence and Impact

**Injury Association:** Scapular dyskinesis has been found in association with almost every pathologic injury in the shoulder and arm in throwing athletes. A meta-analysis found that asymptomatic athletes with shoulder dyskinesis had a 43% higher chance of developing shoulder pain than athletes without scapular abnormalities.

**Performance Impact:** The scapula serves as the stable base for all throwing motions. Proper scapular positioning and movement allow optimal rotator cuff function, efficient force transfer, and reduced stress on the glenohumeral joint.

### Causative Factors for Dyskinesis

Research has identified multiple factors contributing to scapular dysfunction:

1. **Glenohumeral joint injury** - Creates compensatory scapular patterns
2. **Pectoralis minor tightness** - Pulls scapula into protraction and anterior tilt
3. **Lower trapezius weakness** - Reduces upward rotation and depression control
4. **Serratus anterior weakness** - Impairs scapular protraction and upward rotation
5. **Upper trapezius tightness** - Creates excessive scapular elevation

### Evidence for Intervention

**Rehabilitation Outcomes:** Rehabilitation programs that address restoration of scapular mechanics have been shown to decrease rotator cuff symptoms and decrease requests for surgery, both in partial thickness and full thickness tears.

**Performance Enhancement:** Various reports have shown that identification and management of scapular alterations can result in improved rehabilitation and performance outcomes. In healthy athletes, strengthening exercises for scapular stabilizers including the upper and lower trapezius and serratus anterior muscles promote normal scapular movement.

## Scapular Assessment

### Visual Observation (Scapular Dyskinesis Test)

**Static Assessment:**
- Observe scapular position at rest (posterior view)
- Look for winging, asymmetry, or abnormal positioning
- Compare throwing vs. non-throwing shoulder

**Dynamic Assessment:**
- Perform 5 repetitions of weighted arm raises (3-5 lbs)
- Observe for dyskinesis patterns during movement
- Grade as normal, subtle, or obvious dyskinesis

**Common Dyskinesis Patterns:**
1. **Inferior angle prominence** - Lower trap weakness
2. **Medial border prominence** - Serratus anterior weakness
3. **Superior border elevation** - Upper trap dominance

### Scapular Assistance Test (SAT)

**Purpose:** Determines if symptoms are related to scapular position

**Procedure:**
- Manually assist scapula into proper position
- Athlete performs painful movement
- Positive test: Pain reduced with assistance

**Interpretation:**
- Positive SAT suggests scapular dysfunction contributing to symptoms
- Indicates potential benefit from scapular rehabilitation

### Scapular Retraction Test (SRT)

**Purpose:** Assesses strength in retracted position

**Procedure:**
- Test shoulder strength in neutral position
- Manually retract scapula and retest strength
- Compare strength measurements

**Interpretation:**
- Improved strength with retraction: Scapular dysfunction likely
- No change: Look for other factors

## Evidence-Based Scapular Exercises

### Level 1: Foundation and Activation (Weeks 1-3)

#### 1. Low Row
**Target:** Middle trapezius, rhomboids, posterior rotator cuff

**Research Support:** Identified as effective for activating and strengthening scapular stabilizers in both asymptomatic and symptomatic patients.

**Setup:**
- Band anchored at waist height
- Athletic stance, arms extended forward

**Execution:**
- Pull band toward waist, leading with elbows
- Squeeze shoulder blades together at end range
- 15-20 reps, 3 sets
- Daily activation or 5x per week

**Key Points:**
- Retract scapulae, don't just bend elbows
- Maintain neutral spine
- Control return to start position

**Progression:**
- Increase band resistance
- Add 2-second holds at end range
- Perform single-arm variations

#### 2. Scapular Wall Slides
**Target:** Serratus anterior, lower trapezius, upward rotation pattern

**Setup:**
- Stand with back against wall
- Arms at 90 degrees, elbows and hands on wall

**Execution:**
- Slide arms overhead while maintaining wall contact
- Focus on scapular upward rotation
- 12-15 reps, 3 sets
- Daily practice

**Key Points:**
- Keep low back neutral (small gap from wall acceptable)
- Shoulder blades should glide smoothly
- No shrugging or compensation

**Progression:**
- Add resistance band
- Perform away from wall
- Increase to 20 reps

#### 3. Prone Y-T-W Series
**Target:** Lower trapezius, middle trapezius, external rotators

**Setup:**
- Lie face down on bench or stability ball
- Arms hanging toward floor

**Execution:**
- **Y:** Raise arms overhead at 45-degree angle (10 reps)
- **T:** Raise arms straight out to sides (10 reps)
- **W:** Bend elbows, lift hands toward ceiling (10 reps)
- 2-3 sets of complete series
- 3-4x per week

**Key Points:**
- Lead with thumbs up (external rotation)
- Squeeze shoulder blades together
- Slow, controlled movements

**Progression:**
- Add 1-3 lb weights
- Increase reps to 15 each position
- Add 3-second holds

### Level 2: Strengthening and Control (Weeks 4-8)

#### 4. Inferior Glide Exercise
**Target:** Lower trapezius, scapular depression control

**Research Support:** Shown to effectively activate scapular stabilizers and address dyskinesis patterns.

**Setup:**
- Band anchored overhead
- Hold band with arm elevated to 120-140 degrees

**Execution:**
- Pull shoulder blade down and back
- Arm position remains constant
- Feel lower trapezius working
- 12-15 reps, 3 sets
- 3-4x per week

**Key Points:**
- Isolate scapular movement from arm movement
- No shoulder shrugging
- Maintain scapular retraction

**Progression:**
- Increase band resistance
- Add rhythmic stabilization
- Perform in multiple arm positions

#### 5. Lawnmower Exercise
**Target:** Integrated scapular and rotator cuff function

**Research Support:** Identified as effective exercise for scapular stabilizer activation and strengthening.

**Setup:**
- Band anchored at opposite hip level
- Grasp with arm reaching across body

**Execution:**
- Pull band up and across (like starting lawnmower)
- Rotate trunk and retract scapula
- 12-15 reps each side, 3 sets
- 3x per week

**Key Points:**
- Lead with elbow
- Coordinate scapular retraction with trunk rotation
- Control eccentric phase

**Progression:**
- Increase resistance
- Slow tempo to 4 seconds each direction
- Add instability (single-leg stance)

#### 6. Robbery Exercise
**Target:** Scapular retraction and external rotation integration

**Research Support:** Shown effective for activating scapular stabilizers in symptomatic and asymptomatic athletes.

**Setup:**
- Arms elevated overhead in "surrender" position
- Band around wrists or held in hands

**Execution:**
- Pull hands apart while maintaining arm position
- Retract and depress scapulae
- 15-20 reps, 3 sets
- 3x per week

**Key Points:**
- Maintain elevated arm position
- Focus on scapular movement, not just arm
- No neck tension

**Progression:**
- Increase band resistance
- Hold end position for 5 seconds
- Perform during other exercises (overhead squat)

### Level 3: Dynamic Control and Sport-Specific (Weeks 9+)

#### 7. Serratus Punch Plus
**Target:** Serratus anterior, scapular protraction strength

**Setup:**
- Push-up position or standing with band
- Arms extended forward

**Execution:**
- Protract scapulae (push shoulder blades apart)
- "Plus" refers to extra scapular protraction beyond arm extension
- 15-20 reps, 3 sets
- 2-3x per week

**Key Points:**
- Feel ribs expanding apart
- Don't allow scapular winging
- Control throughout full range

**Progression:**
- Push-up position (harder)
- Add push-up between protractions
- Perform on unstable surface

#### 8. Thrower's Ten Exercise Program (Selected Exercises)
**Target:** Comprehensive scapular and rotator cuff integration

**Key Exercises:**
1. External rotation at 0 degrees
2. External rotation at 90 degrees
3. Scapular retraction
4. Scapular protraction
5. Press-ups (serratus emphasis)

**Protocol:**
- 2 sets of 15 reps each exercise
- 3x per week during off-season
- 2x per week in-season maintenance

#### 9. Ball Dribbles on Wall
**Target:** Scapular dynamic stability and proprioception

**Setup:**
- Face wall, small medicine ball in hand
- Arm extended, lean into wall slightly

**Execution:**
- Dribble ball against wall rhythmically
- Allow scapula to protract/retract with dribbles
- 30 seconds each arm, 3 sets
- 2x per week

**Key Points:**
- Relaxed shoulder, let scapula move naturally
- Consistent rhythm
- Progress to faster tempo

**Progression:**
- Increase ball weight
- Vary arm angles
- Add alphabet tracing

## Position-Specific Programming

### Pitchers

**Pre-Throwing Daily (12 minutes):**
1. Scapular wall slides: 15 reps × 2
2. Low row: 15 reps × 2
3. Prone Y-T-W: 10 reps each × 2 sets
4. Inferior glide: 12 reps × 2

**Off-Day Strengthening (30 minutes):**
1. All pre-throwing exercises (increased volume)
2. Lawnmower: 15 reps × 3 each side
3. Robbery: 15 reps × 3
4. Serratus punch plus: 15 reps × 3
5. Ball dribbles: 30 seconds × 3 each arm

**In-Season Maintenance:**
- Pre-throwing routine: Every throwing day
- Strengthening session: 2x per week (non-throwing days)
- Monthly assessment for dyskinesis

### Position Players

**Pre-Practice (10 minutes):**
1. Low row: 15 reps × 2
2. Scapular wall slides: 12 reps × 2
3. Prone Y-T-W: 10 reps each × 1 set
4. Robbery: 15 reps × 2

**Strength Training Days (25 minutes):**
1. All pre-practice exercises (2x volume)
2. Lawnmower: 15 reps × 3 each side
3. Inferior glide: 12 reps × 3
4. Serratus punch plus: 15 reps × 3
5. Thrower's ten selected exercises

## Integration with Shoulder Care

### Sequencing with Other Exercises

**Optimal Order:**
1. Soft tissue work (foam rolling, massage)
2. Scapular activation exercises (wall slides, low row)
3. Rotator cuff activation (band work)
4. Dynamic warm-up
5. Throwing or practice

**Rationale:** Establish scapular stability before loading rotator cuff; proper scapular position optimizes rotator cuff function.

### Combining with Flexibility Work

**Stretch-Then-Activate Approach:**
- Stretch tight muscles (pec minor, upper trap)
- Activate weak muscles (lower trap, serratus)
- Reinforce proper patterns through movement

## Monitoring Progress

### Subjective Markers
- Reduced shoulder fatigue after throwing
- Improved throwing efficiency
- Decreased pain or discomfort
- Better awareness of scapular position

### Objective Markers
- Improved scapular dyskinesis test scores
- Increased strength in scapular exercises
- Enhanced scapular control during movement
- Symmetrical scapular motion bilaterally

### Monthly Reassessment
- Perform scapular dyskinesis test
- Document any asymmetries or dysfunction
- Adjust program based on findings
- Seek professional evaluation if no improvement after 8 weeks

## Common Issues and Solutions

### Issue: Excessive Upper Trap Activation
**Cause:** Compensation for weak lower trap, poor motor control
**Solution:**
- Reduce resistance/load
- Focus on depression cue
- Stretch upper trap before exercises
- Add specific lower trap activation

### Issue: Scapular Winging
**Cause:** Serratus anterior weakness, nerve dysfunction
**Solution:**
- Emphasize serratus-specific exercises
- Progress push-up plus variations
- Rule out long thoracic nerve issues
- Consider professional evaluation

### Issue: No Improvement in Dyskinesis
**Cause:** Insufficient volume, poor exercise selection, structural issues
**Solution:**
- Increase frequency to daily
- Ensure all causative factors addressed
- Consider manual therapy
- Seek sports medicine evaluation

## Conclusion

Scapular mobility and control represent foundational elements of shoulder health in baseball players. With dyskinesis present in 50-100% of injured throwers and a 43% increased risk of pain development, addressing scapular function is essential for injury prevention and performance optimization.

Research consistently supports specific exercises—including the low row, inferior glide, lawnmower, and robbery exercises—for effectively activating and strengthening scapular stabilizers. Rehabilitation programs addressing scapular mechanics have demonstrated success in reducing rotator cuff symptoms and improving outcomes.

By implementing systematic scapular assessment and progressive exercise programming, baseball players can establish the stable, mobile scapular platform required for optimal throwing mechanics. Daily activation (10-12 minutes) combined with 2-3 weekly strengthening sessions provides the stimulus needed to address dyskinesis, prevent injury, and enhance performance. The scapula truly represents the foundation upon which all throwing movements are built.
