# Supplement Guide: What Works, What Doesn't for Baseball

## Overview

The supplement industry markets countless products claiming to improve athletic performance, but which ones actually have scientific support? This evidence-based guide separates effective supplements from expensive placebos, providing baseball players with research-backed recommendations and safety considerations.

## The Evidence Hierarchy: What Research Shows

According to the [International Olympic Committee Consensus Statement](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12498230/), supplements with 'good to strong' evidence of achieving benefits to performance include:

1. **Caffeine**
2. **Creatine**
3. **Inorganic nitrate (beetroot juice)**
4. **Sodium bicarbonates**
5. **Beta-alanine**

Recent research confirms that **caffeine (3-6 mg/kg) consistently improved power output and technical performance in elite athletes**. For baseball players, power output translates directly to bat speed, exit velocity, and throwing velocity.

## Tier 1: Strong Evidence - Recommended

### Creatine Monohydrate

**Evidence level:** Highest - hundreds of studies supporting efficacy

**What it does:**
- Increases muscle mass and strength when combined with resistance training
- Enhances power output for explosive movements (swinging, throwing, sprinting)
- Improves recovery between high-intensity efforts
- May increase body weight through both muscle mass and water retention

**Why it matters for baseball:**
Research shows that [bodyweight has been directly correlated to fastball velocity](https://joshheenan.com/why-all-baseball-players-should-be-using-creatine/), and creatine increases bodyweight through muscle mass and water retention. Among sports with the highest percentage of performance-enhancing product users, baseball ranks prominently, with creatine being particularly popular.

**Dosage:**
- Loading phase (optional): 20g daily (split into 4 doses) for 5-7 days
- Maintenance: 3-5g daily, taken any time of day
- No need to cycle on/off

**Safety:**
- Extensively studied and safe for healthy individuals
- May cause minor water retention (beneficial for pitchers seeking mass)
- Ensure adequate hydration (8-10 glasses water daily)

**Best for:** All baseball players, especially pitchers looking to add mass and power

### Caffeine

**Evidence level:** Highest - consistent performance benefits across studies

**What it does:**
- Increases alertness and focus
- Reduces perceived exertion during exercise
- Enhances power output and technical performance
- Improves reaction time and decision-making

**Why it matters for baseball:**
Baseball requires intense focus for tracking 90+ mph pitches, making quick defensive decisions, and maintaining concentration through 9 innings. Caffeine provides measurable benefits for these cognitive and physical demands.

**Dosage:**
- 3-6 mg per kg body weight
- For 180 lb (82 kg) player: 246-492 mg
- Consume 30-60 minutes before game/practice
- Tolerance develops with daily use

**Sources:**
- Coffee (8 oz): ~95 mg
- Pre-workout supplements: 150-300 mg per serving
- Caffeine pills: 100-200 mg per pill
- Energy drinks: 80-300 mg per can

**Safety considerations:**
- Can cause jitters, anxiety, or sleep disruption in high doses
- Avoid after 2 PM if you have evening games or sleep issues
- NCAA allows caffeine but tests for excessive amounts (>15 mcg/mL urine)
- Start at lower end of dosage range to assess tolerance

**Best for:** All players, particularly for early morning training or when mental focus is critical

### Protein Powder

**Evidence level:** High - well-established for recovery and muscle building

**What it does:**
- Convenient way to meet daily protein requirements
- Supports muscle protein synthesis and recovery
- Helps preserve lean mass during fat loss
- Provides easily digestible post-workout nutrition

**Types:**
- **Whey protein:** Fast-digesting, ideal post-workout (25g protein per scoop)
- **Casein protein:** Slow-digesting, ideal before bed (25g protein per scoop)
- **Plant-based (pea, rice, hemp):** For lactose intolerant or vegan athletes

**Dosage:**
- 1-2 scoops daily as needed to reach total protein target (1.6-2.0 g/kg)
- Not necessary if consuming adequate whole food protein

**Usage patterns:**
In a national survey of about 21,000 U.S. college athletes, respondents reported taking protein products at a rate of 41.7%—the most commonly used supplement among athletes.

**Best for:** Athletes struggling to meet protein needs through whole foods, post-workout convenience

## Tier 2: Moderate Evidence - Consider

### Beta-Alanine

**Evidence level:** Moderate - benefits for repeated high-intensity efforts

**What it does:**
- Buffers muscle acidity during high-intensity exercise
- May improve performance in efforts lasting 1-4 minutes
- Supports training volume and recovery

**Dosage:** 3-6g daily, split into smaller doses to reduce tingling sensation

**Best for:** Players doing high-intensity training; less relevant for in-game baseball performance

### Beetroot Juice / Nitrate Supplements

**Evidence level:** Moderate - benefits for endurance and blood flow

**What it does:**
- Increases nitric oxide production
- May improve blood flow and oxygen delivery
- Some evidence for enhanced endurance

**Dosage:** 300-600mg nitrate (6-12 oz beetroot juice) 2-3 hours pre-exercise

**Best for:** Limited application for baseball; possibly beneficial for conditioning work

## Tier 3: Minimal Evidence - Not Recommended

### BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids)

**Evidence level:** Weak when total protein intake is adequate

**Why not recommended:**
- Whole food protein sources provide all essential amino acids, including BCAAs
- No additional benefit if consuming adequate total protein (1.6-2.0 g/kg)
- More expensive than effective alternatives

**Exception:** May be useful for fasted training or very low-calorie diets

### Testosterone Boosters

**Evidence level:** Negligible for natural athletes

**Why not recommended:**
- No credible evidence supporting natural T-boosters in healthy young athletes
- Often contaminated with banned substances
- Marketing hype far exceeds scientific support

**Better approach:** Ensure adequate sleep, healthy fats, and appropriate training volume

### Fat Burners

**Evidence level:** Weak and potentially dangerous

**Why not recommended:**
- Most ingredients have minimal effect on fat loss
- Often contain excessive stimulants causing jitters and anxiety
- No substitute for proper nutrition and training
- Some contain banned substances

**Better approach:** Follow evidence-based fat loss strategies (moderate caloric deficit, high protein)

## Tier 4: Essential Nutrients (Not Performance Enhancers)

### Multivitamin

**Purpose:** Fill nutritional gaps, not enhance performance

**Who needs it:**
- Athletes with restricted diets (cutting weight)
- Picky eaters with limited food variety
- Those with confirmed deficiencies

**Recommendation:**
- Basic multivitamin without "mega-doses"
- Preferably from whole food sources
- Not necessary if eating varied, nutrient-dense diet

### Vitamin D

**Purpose:** Support bone health, immune function, and recovery

**Who needs it:**
- Athletes training primarily indoors
- Those living in northern latitudes with limited sun exposure
- Anyone with confirmed deficiency (blood test)

**Dosage:** 1,000-4,000 IU daily (based on blood levels and sun exposure)

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (Fish Oil)

**Purpose:** Support anti-inflammatory processes and recovery

**Evidence:** Moderate support for reducing exercise-induced inflammation

**Dosage:** 2-3g combined EPA + DHA daily

**Best for:** Athletes with limited fatty fish intake, pitchers managing arm inflammation

## Safety and Third-Party Testing

The supplement industry is largely unregulated, creating significant risks for athletes.

### The Contamination Problem

Many supplements contain banned substances not listed on the label. This is particularly concerning for:
- High school athletes hoping to play college ball
- College athletes subject to NCAA testing
- Professional prospects subject to Minor League Baseball drug testing

### Third-Party Certification Programs

The [Certified for Sport certification](https://www.nsfsport.com/) is the only independent third-party certification program recognized by:
- United States Anti-Doping Agency (USADA)
- Major League Baseball (MLB)
- National Hockey League (NHL)
- Canadian Football League (CFL)

**Look for these certifications:**
- NSF Certified for Sport
- Informed-Sport
- BSCG Certified Drug Free

**Why it matters:**
Even if you're not subject to drug testing now, future opportunities could require clean test results. Don't risk your career on uncertified supplements.

## Baseball-Specific Supplement Strategy

### For All Players

**Daily foundation:**
- Multivitamin (if diet needs support)
- Vitamin D (2,000 IU, especially winter months)
- Omega-3 (2-3g EPA + DHA)

**Performance optimization:**
- Creatine monohydrate (5g daily)
- Whey protein (as needed to reach 1.6-2.0 g/kg target)

### For Pitchers

**Priority supplements:**
- Creatine (supports mass gain and power)
- Protein powder (easier to hit higher protein targets)
- Omega-3 (anti-inflammatory support for arm health)
- Caffeine (for start-day focus, if tolerated)

**Avoid:**
- Aggressive fat burners or stimulants
- Unproven recovery products
- Anything not third-party tested

### For Position Players

**Priority supplements:**
- Creatine (power and explosiveness)
- Protein powder (recovery convenience)
- Caffeine (game-day focus)

**Optional:**
- Beta-alanine (if doing high-intensity training)

### Pre-Game Supplement Stack

**1 hour before first pitch:**
- Caffeine: 3-6 mg/kg (coffee, pre-workout, or pill)
- Creatine: 5g (if not taking daily, though daily is preferred)
- Light carbohydrate snack

**During game:**
- Electrolyte drink if hot/humid
- No additional supplements needed

**Post-game:**
- Whey protein shake (20-30g)
- Creatine (5g if not taken pre-game)

## Cost-Effective Supplement Approach

Many players waste money on unnecessary products. Here's a budget-prioritized list:

**Tier 1: Best bang for buck ($20-30/month)**
- Creatine monohydrate: ~$15/month
- Basic whey protein: ~$25/month (if needed)
- **Total:** ~$40/month

**Tier 2: Add if budget allows ($40-60/month)**
- Omega-3 fish oil: ~$15/month
- Vitamin D: ~$10/month
- Caffeine (coffee): ~$15/month
- **Total with Tier 1:** ~$80/month

**Tier 3: Optional additions ($60-80/month)**
- Pre-workout with caffeine + beta-alanine: ~$30/month
- Casein protein for before bed: ~$30/month
- **Total with Tiers 1-2:** ~$140/month

**Avoid wasting money on:**
- Proprietary blends with undisclosed ingredients
- Expensive "cutting-edge" supplements with no research
- MLM (multi-level marketing) supplement schemes
- Anything promising unrealistic results

## Red Flags: Supplements to Avoid

Watch for these warning signs:

1. **Proprietary blends** - Hiding actual ingredient amounts
2. **Outrageous claims** - "Gain 20 lbs muscle in 30 days"
3. **No third-party testing** - Risk of contamination
4. **Undisclosed ingredients** - "Proprietary formula"
5. **Aggressive marketing** - Endorsed by non-credentialed influencers
6. **Too good to be true** - If it sounds impossible, it is

## Common Supplement Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Supplement First, Food Second
Relying on supplements instead of whole foods.
- **Fix:** Get 90% nutrition from whole foods; supplements fill gaps

### Mistake 2: Mega-Dosing
Taking 10-20g creatine daily when 5g is sufficient.
- **Fix:** Follow research-based dosages; more isn't better

### Mistake 3: No Third-Party Testing
Buying cheapest option without certification.
- **Fix:** Only purchase NSF Certified for Sport or Informed-Sport products

### Mistake 4: Chasing Trends
Buying every new supplement that hits the market.
- **Fix:** Stick with evidence-based supplements; ignore marketing hype

### Mistake 5: Inconsistent Use
Taking creatine sporadically instead of daily.
- **Fix:** Consistency matters more than timing for most supplements

## Key Takeaways

Only a handful of supplements have strong scientific support for baseball performance: creatine monohydrate (3-5g daily), caffeine (3-6 mg/kg pre-game), and protein powder (to reach daily targets). Creatine is particularly valuable for baseball players seeking to increase power, strength, and mass—qualities directly correlated with pitching velocity and hitting performance. Always choose third-party certified products (NSF Certified for Sport) to avoid contamination with banned substances. Remember that supplements enhance an already solid nutrition foundation—they never replace proper whole food nutrition, adequate sleep, and quality training.
